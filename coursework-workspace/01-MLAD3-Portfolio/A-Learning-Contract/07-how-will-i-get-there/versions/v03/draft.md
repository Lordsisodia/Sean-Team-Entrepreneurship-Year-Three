# MLAD3 Learning Contract - Question 4: How will I get there?

To build my partnership-based AI automation empire, I must develop across six critical competencies that address my specific growth areas. Each connects directly to my exit strategy and reflects honest assessment of where I need to improve. These aren't arbitrary choices—each competency addresses a gap I've identified in myself through difficult experiences with my team.

**Values Alignment** (Self-Development: 4/10 → 8/10)

The core challenge is identifying what truly matters and making decisions consistent with those principles. I've struggled with ethical tension between growth and quality—when David suggested cutting corners on partner vetting to hit targets faster, I felt uneasy but stayed silent, compromising my standards. To develop, I'm clarifying my core values through reflection exercises and creating a decision framework. My target: making values-consistent decisions 80% of the time by February.

**Emotional Intelligence** (Self-Development: 3/10 → 7/10)

I react defensively to feedback. When Harry critiqued my delegation approach in our one-to-one, I felt attacked and immediately shut down, missing valuable insight that could have improved my leadership. This defensive pattern limits my growth and damages relationships. I'm practising the "pause and reflect" technique—taking three breaths before responding to feedback. I aim to respond constructively 70% of the time by March, recognising that feedback is a gift, not an attack.

**Mentoring Others** (Team-Development: 3/10 → 8/10)

I default to doing everything myself because "it's faster than explaining." Working with Sam on partnership outreach, I've felt frustrated when he doesn't approach tasks exactly as I would, then realised I haven't actually taught him my thinking process. This isn't delegation—it's abdication masquerading as efficiency. I'm ethically responsible for developing Sam's capabilities, not just using his output. I'll implement weekly mentoring sessions building his decision-making. I aim for Sam independently managing 50 partnerships by April.

**Team Communication** (Team-Development: 4/10 → 8/10)

Only 40% of my team recommendations are implemented. When Leo challenged my proposal for the partnership structure in our team meeting, I felt dismissed and pushed harder rather than understanding his perspective, which turned potential collaboration into conflict. I've been communicating *at* the team, not *with* them. I'm adopting a "listen first" approach—asking questions before advocating positions. This respects diverse perspectives and creates buy-in. The target is 80% adoption by February.

**Service Design & Delivery** (Venture: 2/10 → 7/10)

I currently have zero agency partnerships despite targeting 1,000. When I presented my partnership model to the team, David pointed out I'd designed it for my convenience, not agency needs—I felt embarrassed but grateful he caught it before I embarrassed myself further. The gap isn't ambition—it's capability. I'm committed to creating genuine value, not extracting from agencies. I'll pilot with 10 agencies using service design frameworks, gathering real feedback before scaling. I aim for 100 validated partnerships by January.

**Financial Management** (Venture: 3/10 → 7/10)

I've focused on building systems without considering revenue capture. When Leo asked about our pricing model, I dodged the question—I felt exposed because I hadn't thought about it. This is a hobby, not a business. I'm shifting to value-capture thinking: understanding unit economics, ethical pricing, and sustainable growth. I'll build a financial dashboard tracking CAC, LTV, and margins weekly. The target is £5K monthly revenue by December, proving the model works.

Timeline: October-November builds foundations (values, emotional intelligence, financial systems), December-February scales what works (mentoring Sam, team communication), March-April integrates all competencies. Every Friday at 4 PM, I review progress—binary metrics that prevent rationalisation.

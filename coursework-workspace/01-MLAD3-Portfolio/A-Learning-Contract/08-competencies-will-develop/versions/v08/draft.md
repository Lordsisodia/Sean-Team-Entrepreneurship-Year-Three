## 1. Values Alignment (Personal: 4/10 → 8/10)

**Competency:** Values Alignment
**Framework:** UWE TE Competency Framework - Ethical & Sustainable Practice: "making decisions aligned with personal and professional values, considering broader ethical implications" (UWE, 2024)

**Definition:** Acting with integrity by making decisions consistent with core principles (integrity, mutual value, long-term focus), even under pressure or when values conflict with expediency. This requires the courage to acknowledge value-behaviour gaps and the discipline to align actions with stated principles.

**Year 2 Gap Analysis:**

My current Values Alignment level (4/10) stems from a documented pattern: I state values that I don't live by. In "Competencies I've Developed," I acknowledged that "I know what I value, but my behaviour often contradicts it." This values-behaviour gap creates ethical tension—I claim integrity while making decisions that undermine trust. For example, I value "team growth" but withhold delegation opportunities. I value "client responsiveness" but prioritise internal optimisation over external outreach. This contradiction damages my credibility with myself and others.

**Why This Development:**

When David suggested cutting corners on partner vetting in November to accelerate partnership acquisition, I felt uneasy but stayed silent to avoid conflict. The specific situation: we had three potential partners who didn't meet our qualification criteria, and David proposed accepting them to hit growth targets faster. I knew this violated our value of "only working with agencies we can genuinely serve well," but I said nothing. This revealed a critical gap: when expediency pressures mount, my values collapse. Sustainable partnerships require a values-based decision framework—I cannot build a 1,000-partnership venture on ethical compromises. The business impact of this gap is already visible: partners who shouldn't have been accepted create service delivery strain and damage our reputation.

**Development Activities:**

1. **Values Clarification Process** (January): Working with TE mentor to articulate my core values explicitly, not implicitly. This includes identifying specific situations where each value was tested and whether I acted consistently.

2. **Decision Framework Creation** (late January): Creating a systematic decision tree for ethical dilemmas. For each significant partnership decision, I will document: (a) What values are involved? (b) What are the short-term expediency pressures? (c) What would values-consistent action look like? (d) Am I choosing expediency over integrity?

3. **Weekly Values Reflection Journal** (starting January): Every Sunday evening, I will review the past week's decisions and rate each for values-consistency using a 1-10 scale. This creates systematic self-awareness about value-behaviour gaps.

4. **Partnership Decision Review** (ongoing): For every partnership decision made in Q1-Q2, I will document it against the values framework and have TE mentor review the documentation biweekly.

5. **Reading & Study**: Completing the UWE TE "Ethical & Sustainable Practice" competency guide and identifying three specific decision-making frameworks used by ethical entrepreneurs.

**Success Indicators:**

- **Primary:** 80% of partnership decisions rated 8+/10 for values-consistency by March 2026 (measured through weekly self-audit journal, reviewed biweekly by TE mentor)
- **Secondary:** Zero instances of staying silent when I notice values violations (tracked through journal documentation)
- **Tertiary:** TE mentor confirms measurable improvement in value-behaviour alignment during biweekly reviews

**Measurement Method:** Weekly self-audit journal where I document (1) decisions made, (2) values involved, (3) pressures felt, (4) action taken, (5) consistency rating. TE mentor reviews biweekly and provides calibration feedback.

**Connection to Exit Strategy:** My goal of 1,000 partnerships cannot be achieved through ethical compromises. Values-consistent partnerships create trust, retention, and referrals—all essential for scaling. Shortcuts now destroy long-term leverage.

## 2. Emotional Intelligence (Personal: 3/10 → 7/10)

**Competency:** Emotional Intelligence
**Framework:** UWE TE Competency Framework - Self-Awareness & Regulation: "recognising and managing own emotional responses, understanding impact on others" (UWE, 2024)

**Definition:** Perceiving, understanding, and managing one's own emotions and relationships—particularly in high-pressure or challenging interpersonal situations. This involves self-awareness (recognising emotional states as they arise), self-regulation (managing emotional responses constructively), and social awareness (understanding how my emotions affect others).

**Year 2 Gap Analysis:**

My current Emotional Intelligence level (3/10) represents my most significant Year 2 failure. In "Competencies I've Developed," I documented that when Harry questioned my AI recommendation, "I defaulted to explaining why I was correct" rather than understanding his concerns. This defensive pattern is systemic. David's feedback—"You're addicted to urgency"—was accurate, but my emotional response was to feel attacked rather than curious. I struggle to receive feedback without becoming defensive, which blocks learning and damages relationships. My INTJ psychometric profile notes preference for independent work, but I use this as an excuse to avoid emotional development. This gap limits my ability to build the trust required for 1,000 partnerships.

**Why This Development:**

The specific incident that revealed this gap: In December, during a team review, Harry critiqued my approach to delegation. He said: "When you delegate tasks but not your thinking, you're not actually delegating—you're setting me up to fail." My immediate emotional response was defensive anger. I thought: "He doesn't understand the complexity of what I'm delegating." I didn't say this, but my body language (crossed arms, tense posture) made my defensiveness visible. The conversation deteriorated from there. This pattern—viewing feedback as attack rather than growth data—repeated throughout Year 2 with Sam, Leo, and David. The business impact: team members stop giving honest feedback because they anticipate my defensive response. This creates an echo chamber where I don't hear critical information needed to improve.

**Development Activities:**

1. **Daily Mindfulness Practice** (started January): 10 minutes daily using Headspace app, specifically the "Responding Rather Than Reacting" module. This builds the pause between stimulus and response.

2. **"Three Breaths" Technique** (immediate implementation): Before responding to any challenging feedback or disagreement, I will take three breaths. This creates physiological space to choose my response rather than reacting automatically.

3. **Weekly Feedback Reflection Journal** (starting January): Every Friday, I will document (a) feedback received that week, (b) my immediate emotional response, (c) whether I responded constructively or defensively, (d) what I wish I had done differently, (e) commitment for next week.

4. **Team Communication Pattern Recording** (February): For two weeks, I will record team meetings and review my communication patterns specifically: (a) Do I ask clarifying questions before advocating? (b) Does my tone invite openness or shut down dialogue? (c) How does my body language affect the conversation?

5. **Reading & Study:** Reading "Search Inside Yourself" by Chade-Meng Tan (emotional intelligence for analytical thinkers) and completing "Crucial Conversations" online course (February).

**Success Indicators:**

- **Primary:** 70% constructive response rate by March 2026 (measured through Harry's weekly feedback logs documenting my response patterns to challenging input)
- **Secondary:** Zero instances where team members withdraw from conversation due to my defensiveness (tracked through team meeting recordings reviewed weekly)
- **Tertiary:** Self-rated emotional regulation during feedback improves from 3/10 to 6/10 (measured through monthly self-assessment rubric)

**Measurement Method:** Harry will maintain a weekly feedback log where he documents (1) feedback given, (2) my immediate response (defensive/open/curious), (3) whether the conversation led to productive outcomes. We will review this together monthly. Additionally, I will self-rate after each feedback conversation using the Emotional Intelligence rubric.

**Connection to Exit Strategy:** Building 1,000 partnerships requires exceptional relationship skills. If I become defensive when partners challenge my approach (which they will), I lose the relationship. High EI enables me to navigate resistance, build trust, and turn conversations toward mutual understanding.

## 3. Mentoring Others (Team: 3/10 → 8/10)

**Competency:** Mentoring Others
**Framework:** UWE TE Competency Framework - Leadership & Team Development: "developing others through effective coaching, delegation, and capability building" (UWE, 2024)

**Definition:** Supporting others' growth by transferring knowledge, skills, and mindsets—moving beyond task delegation to developing team members' independent judgment and capability. This requires shifting from "doing it myself" to "building others' capacity to do it without me."

**Year 2 Gap Analysis:**

My current Mentoring Others level (3/10) is directly connected to my Strategic Leadership gap (5/10). In "Competencies I've Developed," I acknowledged: "When I refuse to let go of control, I'm not just limiting my growth—I'm denying others development opportunities." The portfolio website incident in November exemplified this: I insisted on handling it myself despite Sam being available, creating a two-week delay. But the deeper issue isn't delegation—it's abdication disguised as delegation. I assign tasks without transferring thinking, which leaves Sam operating without guidance. When Sam admitted, "I was guessing at outreach strategy because you'd never documented our qualification criteria," I realised I hadn't actually mentored—I'd abandoned him to figure it out alone. This creates dependency, not capability. My need to feel essential prevents me from building team members who can operate independently.

**Why This Development:**

The business imperative for this development is urgent: I cannot scale to 1,000 partnerships as an individual. The bottleneck is me. When I make all strategic decisions, handle all key client relationships, and control all critical processes, I limit Team Apollo's capacity to my personal bandwidth. This is already impacting growth: in Q4 2025, I turned away three potential clients because I "didn't have capacity"—but the real issue was that I hadn't built Sam's capacity to handle them independently. Sam has been with Team Apollo since September 2024, yet after 16 months, he still cannot independently manage a partnership from initial conversation through closing. This is a leadership failure, not a Sam failure. Year 3 requires shifting from control multiplier to capability multiplier—building team members who can operate at 80% of my effectiveness independently.

**Development Activities:**

1. **GROW Model Implementation** (starting January): Weekly one-hour mentoring sessions with Sam using the GROW framework:
   - **Goal:** What do you want to achieve this week?
   - **Reality:** What's the current situation? What barriers exist?
   - **Options:** What approaches could you take? (I will not provide answers—I will ask questions to generate options)
   - **Way Forward:** What will you do? By when? What support do you need?

2. **Thinking Process Documentation** (January): Creating decision logs where I document my thinking for key decisions (partnership qualification, pricing strategy, negotiation approach). Sam will review these logs to understand my decision-making framework. This transfers thinking, not just tasks.

3. **Independence Assessment Rubric** (January): Creating a 0-2 scale for tracking Sam's independence in partnership management:
   - 0 = Needs my direction for each step
   - 1 = Can execute independently with occasional guidance
   - 2 = Fully independent, consults me only for exceptions

   Sam and I will assess his current level across 5 partnership activities in January and set specific targets for each.

4. **TE Mentoring Workshop** (February): Attending UWE's "Mentoring and Coaching Skills" workshop to learn structured approaches to capability building.

5. **Tolerance Training** (ongoing): When Sam approaches tasks differently than I would, I will explicitly recognise the discomfort and allow his approach. I will document these "tolerance moments" to track my own growth.

**Success Indicators:**

- **Primary:** Sam independently managing 15 partnerships by April 2026 (tracked through weekly independence assessment using the 0-2 rubric across 5 partnership activities)
- **Secondary:** Sam's independence score averages 1.5+ across all partnership activities by March (measured through weekly assessment documentation)
- **Tertiary:** I successfully handle 3+ situations where Sam approaches tasks differently than I would, without intervening or correcting (tracked through tolerance moment documentation)

**Measurement Method:** Every Friday, Sam and I will spend 15 minutes assessing his independence across 5 partnership activities: (1) Initial outreach, (2) Qualification assessment, (3) Proposal presentation, (4) Objection handling, (5) Closing conversation. He will self-rate, I will rate, and we'll discuss differences. This creates clear tracking and shared understanding of what independence looks like.

**Connection to Exit Strategy:** This is the most critical competency for achieving my 1,000-partnership goal. If Sam can independently manage 15 partnerships by April, that's 15 partnerships I don't need to touch. If I can scale this approach to additional team members, my capacity multiplies. The path to 1,000 partnerships is not me working harder—it's building a team of people who can operate at 80% of my effectiveness without me.

## 4. Team Communication (Team: 4/10 → 8/10)

**Competency:** Team Communication
**Framework:** UWE TE Competency Framework - Professional Communication: "influencing stakeholders through strategic communication and relationship management, building trust before sharing expertise" (UWE, 2024)

**Definition:** Communicating strategically to build trust, influence decisions, and manage relationships effectively. This requires recognising that expertise alone doesn't ensure adoption—trust must precede expertise. When team members feel heard, respected, and understood, they become receptive to influence. When they feel lectured, corrected, or overruled, they resist even excellent ideas.

**Year 2 Gap Analysis:**

My current Team Communication level (4/10) directly correlates with my Professional Communication gap (5/10). In "Competencies I've Developed," I acknowledged: "Only 40% of my recommendations are implemented by Team Apollo." This statistic represents a massive communication failure. I provide technically sound recommendations, but team members don't adopt them. Why? Because I communicate in a way that creates resistance rather than receptivity. The pattern is consistent: when Leo challenged my partnership proposal in October, I pushed harder rather than understanding his perspective. I treated his disagreement as a problem to overcome rather than valuable data. When Harry questioned my AI recommendation, I defaulted to explaining why I was correct rather than exploring his concerns. I prioritised being right over being relational. This approach creates opposition—team members dig in their positions because they don't feel heard. The 40% adoption rate isn't a reflection of recommendation quality—it's a reflection of my communication approach.

**Why This Development:**

The specific pattern I need to reverse: I believe that if I can just explain my reasoning more thoroughly, people will agree with me. This is false. In fact, more explanation often creates more resistance. What actually builds receptivity is feeling understood first. When team members feel I understand their perspective before advocating mine, they become open to considering alternatives. This is the core insight from "Crucial Conversations": start with safety, not with data. My current approach starts with data and logic, skipping the safety and relationship foundation. This is why 60% of my recommendations fail. The business impact: miscommunication creates alignment gaps, delayed decisions, and frustrated team members. When Sam, Leo, and David don't adopt my recommendations, we miss opportunities and make preventable mistakes.

**Development Activities:**

1. **Crucial Conversations Course** (February): Completing the full online course, which teaches (a) Start with Heart (know what you really want), (b) Learn to Look (notice when safety is at risk), (c) Make it Safe (describe the gap, not the person), (d) STATE my path (share facts, tell the story, ask for others' paths), (e) Explore Others' Paths (genuine curiosity), (f) Talk Tentatively (share views as possibilities, not certainties).

2. **"Three Questions" Protocol** (immediate implementation): Before advocating for any recommendation, I will ask three clarifying questions:
   - "What's your perspective on this?"
   - "What concerns do you have?"
   - "What would need to be true for this approach to work for you?"

   This ensures I understand others' thinking before sharing my own.

3. **Communication Pattern Recording** (February): For two weeks, I will record team meetings and review specifically for (a) Talk time ratio (am I dominating or balancing?), (b) Question-to-statement ratio (am I asking or telling?), (c) Response to disagreement (do I get defensive or curious?).

4. **Weekly Structured Listening Practice** (ongoing): Every team meeting, I will set an intention to listen more than I speak. I will track my question-to-statement ratio and aim for at least 50% questions.

5. **Recommendation Tracking Dashboard** (January): Creating a simple system to track recommendations made, team response, adoption rate, and time lag. This creates visibility into my communication effectiveness and patterns of resistance.

**Success Indicators:**

- **Primary:** 80% recommendation adoption by April 2026 (tracked through team meeting minutes: recommendations made vs. implemented within 2 weeks)
- **Secondary:** Question-to-statement ratio improves from 20:80 to 50:50 in team meetings (measured through communication pattern recordings reviewed monthly)
- **Tertiary:** Zero instances where team members disengage from conversations I'm leading (tracked through post-meeting self-assessment and peer feedback)

**Measurement Method:** Every team meeting, I will document (1) recommendations made, (2) team response (agreement/concerns/questions), (3) whether the recommendation was adopted within 2 weeks. Monthly, I will review this dashboard with the team and ask: "Are there communication patterns I should change to increase adoption of good ideas?" This meta-communication about communication creates rapid improvement.

**Connection to Exit Strategy:** High team communication effectiveness is essential for scaling. When team members adopt my recommendations quickly, we move faster. When they provide honest feedback that I receive well, we avoid mistakes. Most importantly, strong communication creates the trust required for delegating complex responsibilities—which is the only path to 1,000 partnerships.

## 5. Service Design & Delivery (Venture: 2/10 → 7/10)

**Competency:** Service Design & Delivery
**Framework:** UWE TE Competency Framework - Service Design & Delivery: "creating and delivering value propositions that meet customer needs at scale through systematic processes" (UWE, 2024)

**Definition:** Designing and delivering services that create genuine customer value through systematic processes. This requires moving from "I think agencies need X" to "Agencies have told me they need X." It involves co-creation, rapid prototyping, iterative testing, and measurement of actual value delivered, not just intended value.

**Year 2 Gap Analysis:**

My current Service Design & Delivery level (2/10) represents a fundamental misalignment between my offerings and market needs. I have 70 partnerships despite targeting 1,000—this 7% achievement rate signals that my service design isn't working. When I presented my partnership model to David in November, he identified the core problem: "You've designed this for your convenience, not agency needs." This was painful but accurate feedback. My current approach requires agencies to adapt to my processes rather than adapting my processes to agency workflows. In "Competencies I've Developed," I acknowledged a 75% conversation drop-off rate: four interested prospects, zero closed deals. This closing weakness is a symptom of a deeper problem—I'm not designing services that agencies actually want to buy. The crypto client success (zero disputes, 100% payment completion) demonstrated my service delivery strengths, but the agency partnership failure revealed that I haven't adapted those strengths to the partnership model.

**Why This Development:**

The specific evidence of misalignment: In September 2025, I had conversations with four agency owners who expressed genuine interest in our partnership model. All four conversations ended with "this sounds interesting, let me think about it"—and then zero follow-through. When Sam asked why these prospects weren't closing, I gave vague explanations: "They're probably not ready," or "The timing isn't right." Sam pushed back: "Did you ask them what their actual challenges are? What they're trying to solve?" I hadn't. I had explained my model without understanding their needs. This is backwards: effective service design starts with customer discovery, not solution presentation. The business impact: a 75% drop-off rate means I'm wasting 75% of my sales effort. If I fix this, I triple my closing rate without spending more time on sales.

**Development Activities:**

1. **IDEO/Coursera Service Design Course** (January-February): Completing the full "Design Thinking and Innovation" course, which teaches (a) Empathise (understand user needs), (b) Define (articulate the problem), (c) Ideate (generate solutions), (d) Prototype (build to think), (e) Test (validate with users).

2. **Co-Creation Workshops** (January-February): Scheduling 90-minute sessions with 5 current partners where I will ask (a) "What does your ideal partnership look like?" (b) "What are your biggest frustrations with current service providers?" (c) "What would make this partnership a 10/10 experience?" (d) "What should we start doing, stop doing, and continue doing?" I will document all feedback and identify patterns.

3. **User Journey Mapping** (February): Creating detailed visual maps of the agency onboarding experience from first contact through fully integrated partnership. I will map (a) current state (what actually happens), (b) pain points (frustrations, bottlenecks, confusion), (c) opportunities (where can I add unexpected value?), (d) future state (ideal experience).

4. **Partnership Model Iteration** (March): Based on co-creation insights, I will redesign the partnership model to better align with agency needs. This will involve creating 3 variations and testing them with 10 agencies total, measuring response rates and feedback.

5. **Closing Skills Development** (February): Reading "The Muir Method of Selling" and practising the specific closing conversation flow. Sam and I will role-play closing conversations weekly, and I will track my closing rate improvement.

**Success Indicators:**

- **Primary:** 100 validated partnerships by April 2026 (measured through partnership count + 60-day retention rate; tracked in partnership dashboard)
- **Secondary:** Conversation-to-closing rate improves from 25% to 60% by March (measured through CRM tracking: conversations had vs. partnerships closed)
- **Tertiary:** Co-creation workshops identify at least 3 specific service improvements I can implement immediately (tracked through workshop documentation and implementation)

**Measurement Method:** The partnership dashboard will track (1) conversations had, (2) interest level shown, (3) follow-up actions taken, (4) partnerships closed, (5) partnerships after 60 days (retention). Co-creation workshop insights will be documented and shared with TE mentor for accountability. Monthly, I will review whether implemented service improvements are actually being used and valued by partners.

**Connection to Exit Strategy:** This is the single most important venture competency for hitting my 1,000-partnership goal. My current 7% achievement rate (70 partnerships when targeting 1,000) is not a motivation problem—it's a service design problem. Agencies aren't buying because I'm not selling what they actually need. Co-creation and user journey mapping will reveal what agencies actually value. Closing skills development ensures I capture the value I create. Combined, these activities could triple my closing rate, making 1,000 partnerships achievable.

## 6. Financial Management (Venture: 3/10 → 7/10)

**Competency:** Financial Management
**Framework:** UWE TE Competency Framework - Financial Management: "strategic financial planning and resource allocation for sustainable growth, understanding unit economics and value capture" (UWE, 2024)

**Definition:** Managing financial resources strategically to ensure sustainable venture growth. This requires understanding pricing (what customers will pay), margins (what I keep after costs), customer acquisition cost (how much it costs to acquire a customer), lifetime value (how much a customer is worth over time), and unit economics (does each customer make money or lose money?). Without Financial Management, I have a hobby, not a business.

**Year 2 Gap Analysis:**

My current Financial Management level (3/10) represents a dangerous blind spot. For six months of Year 2, I focused on building systems infrastructure without any revenue. When Leo asked about pricing strategy in December, I felt exposed—because I didn't have one. I couldn't answer basic questions: "What's your target market? What will they pay? What are your margins? What's your CAC? What's your LTV?" The absence of these answers revealed that I'm building a hobby, not a business. In "Competencies I've Developed," I acknowledged that my entrepreneurial judgment (6/10) "consistently overvalues long-term leverage at the expense of immediate value." This pattern—choosing infrastructure investment over revenue generation—created financial risk for the entire team. Sam, Leo, David, and Harry all depend on this venture's financial sustainability. My inability to value-capture threatens their security. This is not just a business problem—it's an ethical problem.

**Why This Development:**

The specific financial reality: In Q4 2025, I spent approximately £2,000 on software, tools, and infrastructure while generating £0 in revenue from the partnership venture. This burn rate is sustainable only if I have external funding or personal savings to cover it—I don't. When Leo challenged me on this in December, he asked: "How long can you keep going without revenue?" I didn't know the answer. This lack of financial visibility is reckless. I need to know (a) How much cash runway do I have? (b) What's my monthly burn rate? (c) When will I run out of money? (d) What revenue milestone do I need to hit to break even? These questions have uncomfortable answers, but not knowing the answers is dangerous. The business impact: without Financial Management, I risk running out of cash and having to shut down or pivot desperately. Without revenue, I cannot validate that the market actually wants what I'm selling.

**Development Activities:**

1. **TE Financial Modelling Workshop** (January): Completing UWE's "Financial Planning for Entrepreneurs" workshop, which covers (a) cash flow forecasting, (b) break-even analysis, (c) unit economics calculation, (d) pricing strategy development, (e) scenario planning (best/worst/most likely cases).

2. **Financial Dashboard Creation** (January): Building a live dashboard tracking:
   - Monthly revenue (target: £5K by April)
   - Monthly expenses (software, tools, contractor costs)
   - Net profit/loss
   - Cash runway (months until cash-out at current burn rate)
   - Customer acquisition cost (CAC)
   - Customer lifetime value (LTV)
   - LTV:CAC ratio (target: 3:1 healthy)

3. **Pricing Strategy Development** (February): Working with an external mentor who has built a £100K+ agency to develop my pricing strategy. This will involve (a) competitor pricing analysis, (b) customer willingness-to-pay interviews, (c) value-based pricing (pricing on outcomes, not hours), (d) tiered packaging options.

4. **Monthly Profitability Review** (January-April): Every month, I will review financial performance with TE coach: (a) Did I hit revenue targets? (b) Are expenses on track? (c) What's the net result? (d) What adjustments needed? (e) What did I learn about unit economics?

5. **Revenue Generation Milestones** (Q1): Setting and hitting specific revenue targets:
   - January: £500 revenue (1-2 small projects or retainers)
   - February: £1,500 revenue (acceleration through referrals)
   - March: £3,000 revenue (consistent monthly recurring revenue)
   - April: £5,000 revenue (stable baseline for scaling)

**Success Indicators:**

- **Primary:** £5K monthly revenue by April 2026 (measured through revenue dashboard with monthly profitability review)
- **Secondary:** Financial dashboard live and tracking all metrics by February (completion of dashboard is a prerequisite for hitting revenue targets)
- **Tertiary:** Pricing strategy developed and tested with 5 prospects by March (measured through proposal acceptance rate at new pricing)

**Measurement Method:** The financial dashboard will automatically pull data from Stripe, bank accounts, and expense tracking tools. I will review this dashboard weekly and share it with TE coach monthly. Revenue will be tracked by client and by month to identify patterns (one-time vs. recurring, high-value vs. low-value). Monthly profitability reviews will produce a one-page summary comparing actual performance to targets and identifying specific adjustments for the following month.

**Connection to Exit Strategy:** Revenue validates the business model. If I can't get agencies to pay for my services, I don't have a business—I have a project. Financial Management ensures I'm building something sustainable, not just something interesting. The £5K monthly target by April is modest compared to my overall goals, but hitting it proves the model works. Once I've proven revenue at £5K/month, scaling to £50K/month becomes execution rather than experimentation. This competency is the foundation for transforming a hobby into a business.

## Integration with Year 3 Development

**Competency Connections:**

The six competencies are strategically interconnected:

- **Values + Emotional Intelligence** → Personal self-awareness foundation. Values alignment requires the emotional stability to act consistently with principles, even under pressure. Emotional intelligence provides the resilience to receive feedback about value-behaviour gaps without collapsing.

- **Mentoring + Communication** → Team capability building. I cannot effectively mentor if I can't communicate without defensiveness. I cannot build team communication effectiveness if I'm hoarding control rather than developing others. These two competencies together transform me from a bottleneck to a force multiplier.

- **Service Design + Financial Management** → Venture sustainability. Service design ensures I'm creating something agencies actually want to buy. Financial management ensures I'm capturing value appropriately and building a sustainable business model. Together, these transform a hobby project into a viable commercial venture.

**Timeline Sequence:**

The competencies develop in phases, with early competencies creating capacity for later ones:

- **January (Foundation Phase):**
  - Values framework creation (Values Alignment)

  - Crucial Conversations course (Communication)

  - Pricing strategy mentorship (Financial Management)

- **March (Application Phase):**
  - Sam managing 15 partnerships independently (Mentoring)

2. **Biweekly TE Mentor Review:** Every two weeks, I will meet with my TE mentor to review progress, discuss challenges, and calibrate self-assessments. The mentor will provide feedback and hold me accountable to development commitments.

3. **Monthly Team Feedback:** At the end of each month, I will ask Sam, Leo, David, and Harry for specific feedback on my development in the team-facing competencies (Mentoring, Communication). I will ask: "What's one thing I did better this month? What's one thing I should work on next month?"

4. **Quarterly Competency Reassessment:** At the end of Q1 (March) and Q2 (June), I will formally reassess my competency levels using the same rubric from Year 2. This creates longitudinal data showing whether development is actually happening or just intended.

5. **Dashboard Tracking:** Quantitative metrics (revenue, partnerships, adoption rates, independence scores, values-consistency ratings) will be tracked in live dashboards for real-time visibility and trend analysis.

Service design and financial management create the sustainable business model. Together, they transform me from an individual with technical skills into a leader building a scalable venture.

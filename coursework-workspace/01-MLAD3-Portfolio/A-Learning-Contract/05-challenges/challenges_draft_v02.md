## CHALLENGE 1: DELEGATION RESISTANCE

When I delegated partnership outreach to Sam in September 2024, I lasted 3 days before stepping back in. I felt anxious watching him make mistakes with his different approach to agency conversations. When Sam's outreach style differed from mine—more conversational where I would be direct—I felt compelled to intervene, telling myself "I'll spend more time explaining than doing it myself."

This rationalization masks my deeper discomfort with losing control over outcomes. The result was predictable: Sam disengaged from the process, I became the bottleneck again, and partnership progress stalled for two weeks. This pattern repeated throughout October and November, where I'd delegate tasks but then micro-manage the execution, preventing team members from learning through doing. My perfectionism prevents others from developing capabilities because I'm not willing to accept short-term mistakes for long-term capacity building. This is not just about productivity—it's about my inability to tolerate the discomfort of watching others learn in ways different from my own.

## CHALLENGE 2: TEAM ADOPTION GAP

I prioritized being technically "right" over building something the team could actually use.

The consequence became clear during client onboarding that same month: when the system needed maintenance, neither Sam nor Pranav could help. We lost 3 days of progress, and clients received delayed responses because the bottleneck was entirely me. I had optimized for my standards rather than team usability. This pattern shows up repeatedly—I build tools that impress technically but create dependency because I don't invest in stakeholder buy-in or usability testing. My mid-range aggressive tendencies mean I push solutions through rather than bringing the team along in the decision process. When I reflect on why I didn't stop to explain the system's architecture or train others, I realize it's because I derive identity from being the technical expert—a status I'd lose if others could do what I do.

The pattern is cyclical: I abandon long-term development for immediate tasks, then feel guilty about not developing, which makes me less effective at the immediate tasks anyway.

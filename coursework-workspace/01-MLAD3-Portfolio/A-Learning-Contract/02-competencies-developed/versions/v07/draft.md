## 6 COMPETENCIES DEVELOPED IN YEAR 2

### 1. Innovation Management (6/10)

Year Two forced development through confronting a values conflict. I spent two days automating 30-minute tasks, justifying this as "leverage beats immediate value." When Sam challenged me in September, asking why I was building automation instead of doing agency outreach, I felt defensive—a signal I needed to confront.

My drive for "systematic innovation" functions as avoidance behaviour. I hide in technical work to escape uncomfortable interpersonal tasks. This perfectionism-as-avoidance caused a six-week delay on our partnership app while I refined automation instead of generating revenue. The competency I developed wasn't about building better systems—it was recognising when optimisation becomes procrastination.

When I optimise systems instead of doing uncomfortable work, I'm prioritising my comfort over team needs. Ethical innovation means balancing systematic thinking with ruthless pragmatism.

---

### 2. Strategic Leadership (5/10)

Year One showed I operated at tactical execution without genuine strategic thinking. Year Two's development came through delegation failures exposing my control issues. Two months ago, building a portfolio website for a major client, I insisted on doing the work myself even though Sam was available. Result: a two-week delay that strained our client relationship. I don't trust others to execute at my standard, so I bottleneck everything at my capacity limit. Leo pointed out this pattern prevents me from operating strategically.

When I refuse to let go of control, I'm not just limiting my growth—I'm denying others development opportunities. The portfolio delay damaged client trust and signalled to Sam that I don't trust his capabilities.

---

### 3. Self-Management (6/10)

Year One identified inconsistent performance patterns. Year Two's Bali pressure forced development, but the real learning came from understanding my relationship with urgency. I thrive under extreme pressure. The community service deadline requiring 19-hour workdays, the fundraising crunch, the visa timeline—all brought out my best performance. But David identified a problematic pattern: I only perform at peak when external forces compel urgency. Without crisis mode, I struggle with consistency.

David challenged me: "You're addicted to urgency." He was right. My best work comes from artificial deadlines I create to simulate crisis.

My urgency addiction raises ethical questions. When I rely on crisis-mode performance, I'm modelling unhealthy work patterns for the team. The 19-hour workdays aren't noble—they're problematic.

---

### 4. Professional Communication (5/10)

Year One showed basic technical communication without genuine stakeholder influence. Year Two's development came through a humbling realisation: being right doesn't mean being influential. When I provided technical guidance to Sam, Leo, David, and Harry, I expected adoption based on technical merit alone. When Harry questioned one of my AI recommendations, instead of understanding his concerns, I defaulted to explaining why I was correct. Result: he didn't adopt the solution—not because it wasn't optimal, but because I hadn't built the relationship foundation making him receptive.

Professional Communication isn't transmitting information—it's building trust so people are receptive when I share expertise. This is why my Year 3 target is 80% team adoption. Moving from 40% to 80% requires recognising trust must precede expertise.

When I dismiss questions because "I'm technically correct," I'm implicitly devaluing others' perspectives. Communication ethics means prioritising relationship over being right.

---

### 5. Entrepreneurial Judgment (6/10)

Year One showed revenue-chasing without strategic resource allocation. Year Two's development came through one decision revealing deeper patterns. We had an opportunity: pursue £50K immediate guaranteed revenue, OR invest that time building BlackBox framework infrastructure. In Year One, I would've chased the revenue. In Year Two, after debate with Leo (favouring revenue) and David (favouring infrastructure), I chose infrastructure.

But this wasn't because I'm inherently strategic. I recognised a pattern: I consistently overvalue long-term leverage at the expense of immediate value. Sometimes this is wisdom—the infrastructure will generate more than £50K over time. But sometimes this pattern is avoidance—fear of uncomfortable short-term execution masquerading as strategic thinking.

When I choose long-term infrastructure over immediate revenue, I'm making a decision that affects everyone's financial security. Ethical judgment requires being transparent about this risk allocation.

---

### 6. Service Design & Delivery (5/10)

Year One identified lack of customer-centric approach. Year Two's development revealed a frustrating pattern: I excel at systematic processes but struggle with execution follow-through—especially closing. The crypto client project demonstrated my strengths: I designed a comprehensive service framework, communicated expectations proactively, managed the relationship well, and achieved zero disputes.

But when pivoting to agency partnerships, a different pattern emerged. In four separate conversations with potential partners, I explained our partnership model, generated genuine interest, then failed to close. The drop-off rate was 75%. Four conversations, four interested prospects, zero closed deals. Sam pointed out what I'd avoided: I create beautiful frameworks but avoid the final step of asking for commitment.

My 75% drop-off rate raises ethical questions about making promises I can't keep. When I generate interest from potential partners but fail to close, I'm wasting their time and potentially damaging our collective reputation. Ethical service delivery means either developing the closing skills to match my design capabilities, or being honest about my limitations and partnering earlier with someone like Sam.

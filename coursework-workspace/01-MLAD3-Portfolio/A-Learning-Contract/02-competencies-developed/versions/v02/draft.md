## 6 COMPETENCIES DEVELOPED IN YEAR 2

### 1. Innovation Management

**Rationale for Development:**

Year One feedback identified my reactive approach to problem-solving without systematic innovation capability. Year Two forced development of Innovation Management, but the real growth didn't come from building more systems—it came from confronting a deep values conflict in my behaviour that I'd been avoiding for years.

I discovered a pattern that both explains and limits my effectiveness: I will spend two days automating a task that would take me 30 minutes manually, consistently justifying this as "leverage beats immediate value." On the surface, this looks like strategic thinking. But when Sam challenged me on this pattern in September, asking why I was building another automation system instead of reaching out to potential partners for our agency work, I felt immediately defensive. That defensiveness was a signal—it forced me to confront an uncomfortable truth about myself.

My drive for "systematic innovation" often functions as avoidance behavior. I hide in comfortable technical work to escape uncomfortable interpersonal tasks like sales conversations, difficult negotiations, or relationship-building outreach. This perfectionism-as-avoidance pattern caused a six-week delay on our partnership app development while I refined automation processes instead of generating revenue. The irony wasn't lost on me: I was optimising systems for a business that wasn't generating any income because I was too busy optimising.

The Innovation Management competency I developed in Year Two wasn't about learning to build better systems—it was about learning to recognise when optimisation becomes procrastination. This required developing self-awareness about my values conflict: I say I value leverage and efficiency, but my behaviour sometimes prioritises comfortable technical work over uncomfortable but necessary business execution.

When I reflect on this pattern, I realise it stems from fear. Sales conversations feel unpredictable; code feels controllable. Agency partnerships require relationship building; automation systems give me clear problems to solve. The technical work is safe—even when it takes two days for a 30-minute task. The business work is scary—even when it's the only path to revenue.

This self-awareness is what I'm bringing into Year 3's Service Design & Delivery work. When we develop partnership models and agency frameworks, I need to balance my systems-thinking strength with actually doing the uncomfortable closing work. Innovation Management for me now means asking: "Is this system serving our business goals, or am I hiding in technical work?" That question alone represents more growth than any automation system I've built.

### 2. Strategic Leadership

**Rationale for Development:**

Year One feedback showed I was operating at tactical execution level without genuine strategic thinking. Year Two's Strategic Leadership development came through a painful, repeated pattern: delegation failures that exposed my control issues in ways I couldn't ignore.

The most revealing example happened two months ago. I was building a portfolio website for a major client while already juggling multiple other client projects. Even though Sam was available, capable, and had capacity to help, I insisted on doing the portfolio work myself. My reasoning felt solid at the time: I had access to all the code, I understood the technical requirements, and I believed I could execute faster and better than anyone else. The result? A two-week delay on delivery that strained our client relationship and damaged trust.

When I reflected on why I couldn't delegate—even when delegating was clearly the strategic choice—I realised my strategic thinking was completely blocked by a control pattern. I don't trust others to execute at my standard, so I bottleneck everything at my own capacity limit. This isn't about quality; it's about control. I'd rather fail through overload than succeed through delegation.

This struggle between "doing it myself for quality" and "delegating for scalability" is the core leadership challenge I'm still working through. Leo pointed out that this pattern actively prevents me from operating at the strategic level because I'm stuck in tactical execution. He was right. If I'm doing the work myself, I'm not thinking strategically about where we need to be in six months—I'm focused on this week's deliverables.

What's particularly frustrating is that I can see the pattern intellectually, but breaking it behaviourally is much harder. Even now, when delegation opportunities arise, my default is still "I'll handle it." It takes conscious effort to pause and ask: "Is this the best use of my time? Could someone else do this well enough? What strategic work am I NOT doing because I'm stuck in execution?"

This connects directly to Year 3's Strategic Leadership priority: I need to develop delegation not as a skill I practice occasionally, but as a necessary leadership behaviour for scaling. The portfolio failure taught me that my control pattern isn't just limiting my leadership—it's actively damaging our client relationships and business potential. Recognising this pattern is the first step. The real work begins now: learning to let go, trusting others to execute, and operating at the strategic level instead of getting stuck in tactical details.

### 3. Self-Management

**Rationale for Development:**

Year One identified poor time management and consistently inconsistent performance patterns. Year Two's Bali relocation pressure forced Self-Management development out of necessity, but the real learning came from understanding my complicated relationship with pressure and urgency.

I discovered something about myself that I'm still processing: I thrive under extreme pressure. The community service deadline that required 19-hour workdays for a week, the fundraising crunch that forced me to learn sales in real-time, the visa timeline that seemed impossible—all of these situations brought out my best performance. But this creates a problematic pattern that David identified during our work together: I only perform at peak level when external forces compel urgency.

Without crisis mode, I struggle with consistency. Day-to-day work without immediate deadlines doesn't trigger the same urgency response, and my performance reflects this. David noticed this pattern and directly challenged me: "You're addicted to urgency." He was right, and hearing it stated so clearly made me confront what this means.

The Self-Management competency I developed in Year Two wasn't about productivity systems or time-blocking techniques—it was about recognising that my best work comes from artificial deadlines I create for myself to simulate crisis. This is both a strength and a limitation. It's a strength because I can perform exceptionally under pressure. It's a limitation because I shouldn't need perpetual crisis to operate at my best.

However, this pattern conflicts with another behaviour I've been developing: when I delegate tasks to AI agents or team members, I often resist because I "want to understand the process" myself. I say this is about quality control, but when I'm honest with myself, it's often about discomfort with not knowing exactly how work is being done. This tension—thriving under pressure I create myself while simultaneously resisting delegation due to control needs—creates a complicated self-management challenge.

This is what I'm working to balance in Year 3, with my target of 80% time alignment with strategic priorities. I need to develop consistent performance without crisis-mode urgency while also learning to delegate effectively despite control discomfort. The Bali pressure proved I can perform at high levels. The challenge now is performing at those levels without requiring external crisis to trigger it.

### 4. Professional Communication

**Rationale for Development:**

Year One feedback showed basic technical communication capability without genuine stakeholder influence. I could explain technical concepts clearly to people who wanted to understand them. But Year Two's Professional Communication development came through a humbling realisation that changed how I think about influence entirely.

The realisation was this: being right doesn't mean being influential.

When I provided technical guidance to the people I live and work with—Sam, Leo, David, Harry—I expected adoption based on technical merit alone. If I explained why a particular AI approach, system architecture, or technical solution was optimal, I assumed people would adopt it because... well, because I was right about the technical details. But I learned through repeated experience that expertise alone ensures absolutely zero influence.

The most revealing moment came when Harry questioned one of my AI recommendations for a project we were working on. Instead of asking questions to understand his concerns, exploring the context he was seeing, or approaching the conversation collaboratively, I defaulted to explaining why I was correct. I treated his question as something to be corrected rather than a perspective to be understood. The result? He didn't adopt the solution—not because it wasn't technically optimal, but because I hadn't built the relationship foundation that would make him receptive to my expertise.

This pattern—technical correctness without relational connection—showed up repeatedly across my work with Sam, Leo, and David as well. I'd have the "right" answer technically, but my communication approach was so directive and non-collaborative that people wouldn't actually use my guidance.

I learned that Professional Communication isn't about transmitting information—it's about building trust so that when I share expertise, people are actually receptive to hearing it. The transmission part is easy. The trust-building part is what I'd been missing entirely. Being technically correct is meaningless if I haven't built the relationship that makes my expertise useful to others.

This is why my Year 3 target is 80% team adoption, not just "more communication." The metric isn't output—how much I communicate. The metric is influence—whether people actually adopt and use what I share. Moving from 40% to 80% adoption requires recognising that trust must precede expertise, relationships must precede recommendations, and collaboration must precede correction. This communication competency isn't just professional—it's deeply personal, and it requires me to show up differently in every conversation.

### 5. Entrepreneurial Judgment

**Rationale for Development:**

Year One showed clear revenue-chasing behaviour without strategic resource allocation thinking. I prioritised immediate revenue over long-term positioning, which meant constant tactical execution without genuine strategic building. Year Two's Entrepreneurial Judgment development came through one specific decision that revealed deeper patterns in my decision-making.

The situation: we had an opportunity to pursue £50K in immediate, guaranteed revenue from an existing client, OR we could invest that same time building our BlackBox framework infrastructure. The revenue was tangible, immediate, and certain. The infrastructure investment was speculative, long-term, and uncertain.

In Year One, I would have chased the revenue without hesitation. Guaranteed £50K is hard to turn down when you're thinking tactically. But in Year Two, after weeks of debate with Leo (who strongly favoured the revenue) and David (who favoured the infrastructure), I ultimately chose the infrastructure path.

What's interesting is that this wasn't because I'm inherently strategic or because I've mastered long-term thinking. It was because I recognised a pattern in my behaviour: I consistently overvalue long-term leverage at the expense of immediate value delivery. Sometimes this is wisdom—the infrastructure investment will likely generate far more than £50K over time. But sometimes this pattern is actually avoidance—fear of uncomfortable short-term execution masquerading as strategic long-term thinking.

The real judgment call wasn't the infrastructure choice itself. It was recognising that my default preference for "leverage" might sometimes be fear of immediate business pressures. Pursuing £50K in revenue requires sales conversations, client meetings, delivery work—all the uncomfortable interpersonal stuff I've been hiding from. Building infrastructure is comfortable technical work that feels strategic but might actually be avoidance.

This self-awareness about my decision-making patterns is what I'm bringing into Year 3's priority management development. When I'm making decisions about speculative AI development versus immediate partnership revenue, I need to ask: Is this genuinely strategic leverage, or am I avoiding uncomfortable execution? The answer isn't always obvious, but asking the question represents significant growth from Year One's automatic revenue-chasing.

Entrepreneurial Judgment for me now means recognising my patterns, questioning my defaults, and making decisions from conscious awareness rather than reactive habit. Sometimes I'll still choose infrastructure over revenue—but now I know why I'm making that choice, and I can be honest with myself about whether it's wisdom or avoidance.

### 6. Service Design & Delivery

**Rationale for Development:**

Year One feedback identified a lack of customer-centric approach to service delivery. I was solving technical problems well, but I wasn't thinking in terms of service experiences or client journeys. Year Two's Service Design & Delivery development revealed a frustrating pattern that explains both my strengths and my limitations: I excel at creating systematic processes, but I struggle with execution follow-through—especially the closing.

The crypto client project demonstrated my strengths clearly. I designed a comprehensive service framework, communicated expectations proactively, managed the relationship well throughout delivery, and achieved zero disputes. The systems-thinking piece of service design is natural for me. I can create beautiful processes that work smoothly.

But when we pivoted to agency partnerships, a different pattern emerged that exposed my weakness. In four separate conversations with potential agency partners, I successfully explained our partnership model, generated genuine interest, and then... failed to close the deal. I could do the design work and the communication work, but I struggled with the execution work of actually asking for commitment.

The drop-off rate was 75%. Four conversations, four interested prospects, zero closed deals. The pattern became impossible to ignore: excellent systems design, weak execution follow-through.

Sam, who excels at relationship closing and direct execution, pointed out what I'd been avoiding: I create beautiful frameworks but avoid the final step of asking for commitment. I can design the partnership model, I can communicate it clearly, I can generate interest—but when it's time to actually close, I freeze.

This pattern—great systems thinking, weak execution closing—appears across my work. I'm strong at design, communication, and planning. I'm weak at direct execution, closing, and follow-through. Service Design & Delivery in Year Two wasn't about learning to design services—I already know how to do that. It was about confronting that my strength in systematic processes masks a genuine weakness in direct execution.

This self-awareness is what I'm bringing into Year 3's partnership model development. When we design agency frameworks and partnership structures, I need to balance my systems strength with practicing the uncomfortable closing conversations I've been avoiding. Sam can model the closing behaviour, but I need to actually practice it myself. Service design isn't enough—I need to develop the full cycle: design, communicate, AND execute the closing.

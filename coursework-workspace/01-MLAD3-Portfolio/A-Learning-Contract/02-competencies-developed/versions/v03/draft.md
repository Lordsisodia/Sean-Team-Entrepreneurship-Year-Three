## 6 COMPETENCIES DEVELOPED IN YEAR 2

### 1. Innovation Management

**Rationale for Development:**

Year Two forced development of Innovation Management through confronting a values conflict I'd avoided: I spend two days automating 30-minute tasks, justifying this as "leverage beats immediate value." When Sam challenged me in September, asking why I was building automation instead of doing agency outreach, I felt defensive—a signal I needed to confront.

My drive for "systematic innovation" functions as avoidance behavior. I hide in technical work to escape uncomfortable interpersonal tasks like sales conversations. This perfectionism-as-avoidance caused a six-week delay on our partnership app while I refined automation instead of generating revenue. I was optimising systems for a business generating zero income because I was too busy optimising.

The competency I developed wasn't about building better systems—it was recognising when optimisation becomes procrastination. I say I value leverage and efficiency, but my behaviour prioritises comfortable technical work over uncomfortable business execution. This stems from fear: sales conversations feel unpredictable; code feels controllable.

Innovation Management now means asking: "Is this system serving our goals, or am I hiding in technical work?" That question represents more growth than any automation system I've built.

### 2. Strategic Leadership

**Rationale for Development:**

Year One showed I operated at tactical execution without genuine strategic thinking. Year Two's Strategic Leadership development came through delegation failures exposing my control issues.

Two months ago, building a portfolio website for a major client, I insisted on doing the work myself even though Sam was available and capable. My reasoning: I understood the technical requirements and could execute faster. Result: a two-week delay that strained our client relationship.

When I reflected on why I couldn't delegate, I realised my strategic thinking was blocked by a control pattern. I don't trust others to execute at my standard, so I bottleneck everything at my capacity limit. I'd rather fail through overload than succeed through delegation. Leo pointed out this pattern prevents me from operating strategically because I'm stuck in tactical execution.

The portfolio failure taught me my control pattern isn't just limiting my leadership—it's actively damaging our client relationships and business potential. Recognising this is the first step. The real work: learning to let go, trusting others to execute, and operating strategically instead of getting stuck in tactical details.

### 3. Self-Management

**Rationale for Development:**

Year One identified inconsistent performance patterns. Year Two's Bali pressure forced Self-Management development, but the real learning came from understanding my relationship with urgency.

I thrive under extreme pressure. The community service deadline requiring 19-hour workdays, the fundraising crunch, the visa timeline—all brought out my best performance. But David identified a problematic pattern: I only perform at peak when external forces compel urgency. Without crisis mode, I struggle with consistency.

David challenged me: "You're addicted to urgency." He was right. My best work comes from artificial deadlines I create to simulate crisis. This is both strength and limitation: I can perform exceptionally under pressure, but I shouldn't need perpetual crisis to operate at my best.

However, this pattern conflicts with another behaviour: when delegating to AI agents or team members, I resist because I "want to understand the process" myself. I say it's about quality control, but it's often discomfort with not knowing exactly how work is being done.

This tension—thriving under self-created pressure while resisting delegation due to control needs—creates a complicated challenge. I need to develop consistent performance without crisis-mode urgency while learning to delegate despite control discomfort.

### 4. Professional Communication

**Rationale for Development:**

Year One showed basic technical communication without genuine stakeholder influence. Year Two's development came through a humbling realisation: being right doesn't mean being influential.

When I provided technical guidance to Sam, Leo, David, Harry, I expected adoption based on technical merit alone. If I explained why an AI approach or technical solution was optimal, I assumed people would adopt it because I was right. But expertise alone ensures zero influence.

When Harry questioned one of my AI recommendations, instead of understanding his concerns, I defaulted to explaining why I was correct. I treated his question as something to correct rather than a perspective to understand. Result: he didn't adopt the solution—not because it wasn't optimal, but because I hadn't built the relationship foundation making him receptive.

This pattern—technical correctness without relational connection—showed up repeatedly. I'd have the "right" answer, but my directive, non-collaborative approach meant people wouldn't use my guidance.

Professional Communication isn't transmitting information—it's building trust so people are receptive when I share expertise. Being technically correct is meaningless without the relationship making my expertise useful to others.

This is why my Year 3 target is 80% team adoption, not "more communication." The metric isn't output but influence. Moving from 40% to 80% requires recognising trust must precede expertise, relationships must precede recommendations, collaboration must precede correction.

### 5. Entrepreneurial Judgment

**Rationale for Development:**

Year One showed revenue-chasing without strategic resource allocation. I prioritised immediate revenue over long-term positioning. Year Two's Entrepreneurial Judgment came through one decision revealing deeper patterns.

We had an opportunity: pursue £50K immediate guaranteed revenue, OR invest that time building BlackBox framework infrastructure. In Year One, I would've chased the revenue. In Year Two, after weeks of debate with Leo (favouring revenue) and David (favouring infrastructure), I chose infrastructure.

But this wasn't because I'm inherently strategic. I recognised a pattern: I consistently overvalue long-term leverage at the expense of immediate value. Sometimes this is wisdom—the infrastructure will generate more than £50K over time. But sometimes this pattern is avoidance—fear of uncomfortable short-term execution masquerading as strategic thinking.

The real judgment wasn't the infrastructure choice. It was recognising my default preference for "leverage" might be fear of immediate business pressures. Pursuing £50K requires sales conversations, client meetings, delivery work—the uncomfortable interpersonal stuff I hide from. Building infrastructure is comfortable technical work feeling strategic but might be avoidance.

Entrepreneurial Judgment now means recognising my patterns, questioning my defaults, making decisions from conscious awareness rather than reactive habit. Sometimes I'll choose infrastructure over revenue—but now I know why and can be honest whether it's wisdom or avoidance.

### 6. Service Design & Delivery

**Rationale for Development:**

Year One identified lack of customer-centric approach to service delivery. Year Two's development revealed a frustrating pattern explaining my strengths and limitations: I excel at systematic processes but struggle with execution follow-through—especially closing.

The crypto client project demonstrated my strengths: I designed a comprehensive service framework, communicated expectations proactively, managed the relationship well, achieved zero disputes. Systems-thinking is natural for me.

But when pivoting to agency partnerships, a different pattern emerged. In four separate conversations with potential partners, I explained our partnership model, generated genuine interest, then failed to close. I could do design and communication work, but struggled with execution—actually asking for commitment.

The drop-off rate was 75%. Four conversations, four interested prospects, zero closed deals. Sam, who excels at closing, pointed out what I'd avoided: I create beautiful frameworks but avoid the final step of asking for commitment.

This pattern—great systems thinking, weak execution closing—appears across my work. I'm strong at design, communication, planning. I'm weak at direct execution, closing, follow-through. Service Design & Delivery wasn't learning to design services—I already know that. It was confronting my strength in systematic processes masks genuine weakness in direct execution.

This self-awareness guides Year 3's partnership model development. I need to balance systems strength with practicing uncomfortable closing conversations. Sam can model the behaviour, but I need to practice it myself.

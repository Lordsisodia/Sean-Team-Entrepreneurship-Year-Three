## 6 COMPENCIES DEVELOPED IN YEAR 2

### 1. Innovation Management

**Current Level: 6/10**

**Personal Experience:**

Year Two forced development through confronting a values conflict: I spent two days automating 30-minute tasks, justifying this as "leverage beats immediate value." When Sam challenged me in September, asking why I was building automation instead of doing agency outreach, I felt defensive—a signal I needed to confront.

My drive for "systematic innovation" functions as avoidance behaviour. I hide in technical work to escape uncomfortable interpersonal tasks like sales conversations. This perfectionism-as-avoidance caused a six-week delay on our partnership app while I refined automation instead of generating revenue. I was optimising systems for a business generating zero income because I was too busy optimising.

The competency I developed wasn't about building better systems—it was recognising when optimisation becomes procrastination. Innovation Management now means asking: "Is this system serving our goals, or am I hiding in technical work?"

**Values & Ethics Dimension:**

When I optimise systems instead of doing uncomfortable work, I'm prioritising my comfort over team needs. The partnership app delay prevented Sam from moving forward and delayed our revenue timeline. Ethical innovation means balancing systematic thinking with ruthless pragmatism, recognising that my pursuit of "perfect systems" can become selfish when it blocks team progress.

### 2. Strategic Leadership

**Current Level: 5/10**

**Personal Experience:**

Year One showed I operated at tactical execution without genuine strategic thinking. Year Two's development came through delegation failures exposing my control issues.

Two months ago, building a portfolio website for a major client, I insisted on doing the work myself even though Sam was available and capable. Result: a two-week delay that strained our client relationship. I don't trust others to execute at my standard, so I bottleneck everything at my capacity limit. Leo pointed out this pattern prevents me from operating strategically because I'm stuck in tactical execution.

The portfolio failure taught me my control pattern isn't just limiting my leadership—it's actively damaging our client relationships. The real work: learning to let go, trusting others to execute, and operating strategically instead of getting stuck in tactical details.

**Values & Ethics Dimension:**

When I refuse to let go of control, I'm not just limiting my growth—I'm denying others development opportunities and burning myself out in ways that hurt the team. The portfolio delay damaged client trust and signalled to Sam that I don't trust his capabilities, undermining team psychological safety.

---

### 3. Self-Management

**Current Level: 6/10**

**Personal Experience:**

Year One identified inconsistent performance patterns. Year Two's Bali pressure forced development, but the real learning came from understanding my relationship with urgency.

I thrive under extreme pressure. The community service deadline requiring 19-hour workdays, the fundraising crunch, the visa timeline—all brought out my best performance. But David identified a problematic pattern: I only perform at peak when external forces compel urgency. Without crisis mode, I struggle with consistency.

David challenged me: "You're addicted to urgency." He was right. My best work comes from artificial deadlines I create to simulate crisis. This is both strength and limitation: I can perform exceptionally under pressure, but I shouldn't need perpetual crisis to operate at my best.

**Values & Ethics Dimension:**

My urgency addiction raises ethical questions about sustainable work practices. When I rely on crisis-mode performance, I'm modelling unhealthy work patterns for the team. The 19-hour workdays aren't noble—they're problematic. Ethical self-management means developing consistency without requiring crisis, both for my own wellbeing and to model healthy practices for the team.

### 4. Professional Communication

**Current Level: 5/10**

**Personal Experience:**

Year One showed basic technical communication without genuine stakeholder influence. Year Two's development came through a humbling realisation: being right doesn't mean being influential.

When I provided technical guidance to Sam, Leo, David, and Harry, I expected adoption based on technical merit alone. When Harry questioned one of my AI recommendations, instead of understanding his concerns, I defaulted to explaining why I was correct. Result: he didn't adopt the solution—not because it wasn't optimal, but because I hadn't built the relationship foundation making him receptive.

Professional Communication isn't transmitting information—it's building trust so people are receptive when I share expertise. Being technically correct is meaningless without the relationship making my expertise useful to others.

This is why my Year 3 target is 80% team adoption, not "more communication." The metric isn't output but influence. Moving from 40% to 80% requires recognising trust must precede expertise.

**Values & Ethics Dimension:**

When I dismiss questions because "I'm technically correct," I'm implicitly devaluing others' perspectives and claiming intellectual superiority. Harry's question wasn't a challenge to be overcome—it was a perspective to engage with. My failure to recognise this damaged trust. Communication ethics means prioritising relationship over being right.

### 5. Entrepreneurial Judgment

**Current Level: 6/10**

**Personal Experience:**

Year One showed revenue-chasing without strategic resource allocation. Year Two's development came through one decision revealing deeper patterns.

We had an opportunity: pursue £50K immediate guaranteed revenue, OR invest that time building BlackBox framework infrastructure. In Year One, I would've chased the revenue. In Year Two, after debate with Leo (favouring revenue) and David (favouring infrastructure), I chose infrastructure.

But this wasn't because I'm inherently strategic. I recognised a pattern: I consistently overvalue long-term leverage at the expense of immediate value. Sometimes this is wisdom—the infrastructure will generate more than £50K over time. But sometimes this pattern is avoidance—fear of uncomfortable short-term execution masquerading as strategic thinking.

Entrepreneurial Judgment now means recognising my patterns, questioning my defaults, and making decisions from conscious awareness rather than reactive habit.

**Values & Ethics Dimension:**

When I choose long-term infrastructure over immediate revenue, I'm not just making a strategic decision—I'm making a decision that affects everyone's financial security. The £50K would have provided immediate stability for the team. By choosing infrastructure, I'm implicitly asking others to share the risk of my speculative bet. Ethical judgment requires being transparent about this risk allocation.

### 6. Service Design & Delivery

**Current Level: 5/10**

**Personal Experience:**

Year One identified lack of customer-centric approach. Year Two's development revealed a frustrating pattern: I excel at systematic processes but struggle with execution follow-through—especially closing.

The crypto client project demonstrated my strengths: I designed a comprehensive service framework, communicated expectations proactively, managed the relationship well, and achieved zero disputes. Systems-thinking is natural for me.

But when pivoting to agency partnerships, a different pattern emerged. In four separate conversations with potential partners, I explained our partnership model, generated genuine interest, then failed to close. The drop-off rate was 75%. Four conversations, four interested prospects, zero closed deals. Sam pointed out what I'd avoided: I create beautiful frameworks but avoid the final step of asking for commitment.

Service Design & Delivery wasn't learning to design services—I already know that. It was confronting that my strength in systematic processes masks genuine weakness in direct execution.

**Values & Ethics Dimension:**

My 75% drop-off rate raises ethical questions about making promises I can't keep. When I generate interest from potential partners but fail to close, I'm not just failing myself—I'm wasting their time and potentially damaging our collective reputation. Ethical service delivery means either developing the closing skills to match my design capabilities, or being honest about my limitations and partnering earlier with someone like Sam who can execute the full cycle.

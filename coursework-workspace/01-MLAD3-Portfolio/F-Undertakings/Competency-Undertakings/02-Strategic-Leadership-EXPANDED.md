## Competency

**What is the name and definition of this competency?**

Strategic Leadership: The ability to envision and communicate a compelling future, build and develop high-performing teams, and create systems that enable scalable organisational success through others rather than individual effort.

**How is this competency relevant to my situation and SMART Objectives?**

Strategic leadership is critical because scaling from individual effort to team-based delivery requires learning to lead through systems, not just direct involvement. The evidence of my leadership development is a documented failure that produced profound learning.

From December 10, 2025 to January 20, 2026, I attempted to delegate infrastructure setup and e-commerce client acquisition to Sam. I implemented a tracking system with £2.49/day fines for missed task tracking. After 60 days, both of us owed hundreds of pounds in fines, so we wrote off the system. This leadership failure taught me a critical lesson: "When systems fail, look at system design before blaming people." Sam successfully completed all assigned work - I never had to redo anything. The problem wasn't Sam's capability; it was my burdensome tracking system.

This experience demonstrates strategic leadership through vulnerability: acknowledging my design failure, learning that good systems make the right work easy (not just track wrong work), and recognising that Sam's capability validates the delegation strategy even if my first system failed.

SMART Objective 3 targets team-based delivery. The delegation attempt was the first step toward that goal, and the failure provided the systems-thinking foundation required for successful scaling.

## Progression through Practice

I progressed in strategic leadership through a hands-on delegation experience with verifiable outcomes.

**Delegation Attempt (December 10-30, 2025):**
I attempted to hand off infrastructure setup and e-commerce client acquisition to Sam. I identified two operational areas that could be delegated: (1) technical infrastructure setup for new partners, and (2) e-commerce client acquisition and onboarding. I created initial handoff documentation. This connected to Strategic Leadership by demonstrating recognition of the need to delegate operational work to focus on strategic priorities.

**Tracking System Implementation (December 15, 2025):**
I implemented a daily task tracking system with £2.49/day fine for missed tracking. The theory was that financial accountability would ensure compliance. Both Sam and I would track our daily tasks, and any day we missed tracking we owed £2.49 to a shared pool. This connected to behavioral design theory (BJ Fogg, from LfO-06 research) - I was trying to use consequences (financial penalty) to drive behavior.

**System Failure (December 15, 2025 - January 15, 2026):**
Both Sam and I found the tracking system burdensome. We frequently forgot to track. The fines accumulated: after 60 days, we both owed several hundred pounds. The system created resentment rather than compliance. This connected to management theory from "High Output Management" - accountability systems should enable performance, not create bureaucracy. I had made the classic management mistake: prioritizing control (tracking) over usability (ease of use).

**Recognition of Sam's Capability (ongoing throughout):**
Despite the tracking system failure, Sam successfully completed all assigned infrastructure and client acquisition work. I never had to redo his work or fix his mistakes. This revealed: Sam is capable but the tracking system was the problem, not Sam's performance. This connected to trust vs. control dynamics - Sam's capability proved I can trust him with real work.

**System Abandonment and Acknowledgment (January 15-20, 2026):**
After 60 days, we agreed to write off the system and the fines. I acknowledged that the tracking system was my failure as a leader, not Sam's failure as a team member. This honest admission builds trust and demonstrates vulnerable leadership.

**Process Documentation Shift (January 20 onwards):**
Instead of complex tracking systems, I began focusing on documenting standard operating procedures for tasks Sam is handling. The goal is to create clear processes that are easy to follow, not burdensome tracking that feels like policing. This connected to systems thinking from "The Phoenix Project" (documented in LfO-03) - good systems make the right work easy, not just track the wrong work.

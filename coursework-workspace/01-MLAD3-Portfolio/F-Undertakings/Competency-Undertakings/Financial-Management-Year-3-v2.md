## Competency

**What is the name and definition of this competency?**

Financial Management: The ability to understand, analyse, and make decisions about financial resources to ensure business sustainability and growth. This encompasses pricing strategy, unit economics analysis, revenue modeling, and making evidence-based financial decisions that support scaling.

**How is this competency relevant to my situation and SMART Objectives?**

Financial management is critical to my Year 3 business strategy. SMART Objective 6 targets scaling from 100 to 500 partnerships, progressing from £50K to £250K monthly revenue. This requires validating unit economics, pricing models, and scaling projections.

This competency also connects to strategic team building. The first partnership manager hire (targeted January 2026) is enabled by the £50K monthly revenue from 100 partnerships. This is bootstrap financing: revenue funds growth.

## Key Learning

One significant learning moment came from studying pricing strategy through LfO-05. I initially considered cost-plus pricing but shifted to value-based pricing: price based on value received, not cost incurred. The £500/month partnership price represents roughly 10x ROI for partners. The insight was: "Pricing isn't about what it costs you—it's about what value the customer receives. Value-based pricing maximises both revenue and satisfaction."

Another key learning came from unit economics analysis. Before scaling to 500 partnerships, I needed to validate unit economics: Customer Acquisition Cost (CAC), Customer Lifetime Value (LTV), CAC:LTV ratio. The analysis showed positive unit economics. The insight was: "Business model validation requires actual data, not projections. Unit economics must be positive before scaling commitment."

A third insight emerged from understanding the bootstrap financing model. The first partnership manager hire is funded by £50K monthly revenue from 100 partnerships. This is bootstrap: business success funds team expansion, which enables further scaling. The alternative would be external investment. The insight was: "Bootstrap financing forces unit economics discipline. Validate first, scale second."

A fourth learning moment came from reviewing the 500-partner financial projections. £250K monthly revenue from 500 partnerships sounds impressive, but the question is whether unit economics hold at scale. The insight was: "Scaling projections are only as good as the unit economics they're built on. If economics work at 100 partners, they'll likely work at 500. If economics are marginal at 100, they won't improve at scale."

These experiences collectively shaped my understanding of financial management. I moved from viewing financial decisions as "necessary but secondary" to viewing them as "foundational to business strategy."

## Progression through Enquiry

Enquiry-based learning was essential for developing financial management capability. The LfO-05 undertaking was systematic study of financial thinking, pricing strategy, and business models.

Key frameworks studied:

**Value-Based Pricing:** Price based on value delivered to customer, not cost to produce. For partnerships, minimum value is £5K/month. Pricing at £500/month means partner gets 10x ROI.

**Unit Economics (CAC:LTV):** Healthy SaaS businesses have CAC:LTV of 1:3 or better. Unit economics determine whether business model scales or fails.

**Bootstrap vs Venture Funding:** Bootstrap forces unit economics discipline—you can't scale without positive economics. Venture funding can mask bad economics. Bootstrap is slower but more sustainable.

**SaaS Financial Modeling:** SaaS businesses have predictable economics if unit economics are positive. Revenue = customers × price. Complexity is in acquisition and retention.

**Evidence-Based Scaling:** Most common startup mistake is premature scaling. Lean Startup approach: validate at small scale before large scale.

How this influenced my professional values and practices:

Financial research reinforced commitment to evidence-based decision-making. Old approach: set ambitious targets, figure out execution. New approach: validate at small scale, commit to scale. The 100-partnership target is validation; 500-partnership target is scaling commitment.

I also developed appreciation for financial foundation of business strategy. Product, marketing, operations—all important. But financial validation is prerequisite for scaling.

By integrating this knowledge, I've built a validated business model. The 100-partnership target validates core model. The 500-partnership target builds on validated economics.

However, financial management is ongoing development. True test: will economics hold as scale increases to 250 and 500 partners?

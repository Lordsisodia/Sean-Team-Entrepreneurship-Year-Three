## Competency

**What is the name and definition of this competency?**

Self-Management: The ability to regulate one's emotions, thoughts, and behaviors effectively, including managing stress, maintaining discipline, building sustainable habits, and developing personal capacity to enable professional success. This encompasses habit formation, time management, energy management, and honest self-assessment.

**How is this competency relevant to my situation and SMART Objectives?**

Self-management is the foundation that enables all other competencies. SMART Objective 1 (80%+ cannabis-free days, 50+ focused hours/week) and Objective 2 (time-tracking system for capacity planning) both target self-management capability. The 40% and 85% progress ratings respectively reflect both progress and the gap between intention and execution.

This competency is critical because partnership scaling (100→500 partners) demands sustained high performance. The 100-partnership milestone achieved in April 2026 required significant personal investment—manual outreach at 60 conversations/week, client app delivery, team development. Scaling to 500 partnerships will require even greater capacity. Self-management isn't a nice-to-have—it's the enabling constraint on what I can achieve.

The connection between Year 2 and Year 3 is significant. In Year 2, I successfully quit smoking through habit change principles—this demonstrated that I could design systems for personal transformation. In Year 3, I'm applying those same principles to professional capacity: designing systems (time-tracking app), setting realistic targets (80% vs 100%), and building sustainable habits (6:30 AM wake routine, 50+ focused hours/week).

The app revealed a different reality: ~50% actual compliance vs the 100% target I believed I was hitting. This was confronting but valuable. These adjustments demonstrate Entrepreneurial Judgment through realistic self-assessment.

Another key learning came from recognising the difference between maximum capacity and sustainable capacity. In October-November 2025, I attempted to work 70+ focused hours per week while managing partnership scaling. I experienced early signs of burnout—diminishing returns, fatigue, reduced quality. This isn't about laziness—it's about sustainability. The 100-partnership achievement (April 2026) was accomplished with this sustainable approach, validating that quality matters more than quantity.

A third insight emerged from the 6:30 AM wake routine practice. I established this routine in October 2025, aiming for 100% consistency. The morning routine (meditation → exercise → planning → deep work start) enables the 50 focused hours/week target. The routine is the foundation; the specific activities can vary.

A fourth learning moment came from connecting Year 2 smoking cessation to Year 3 habit management. In Year 2, I successfully quit smoking through environmental design (Bali environment supporting habit change) and systematic tracking. In Year 3, I'm applying the same principles to professional habits: time-tracking app for self-awareness, 6:30 AM routine for environmental consistency, realistic targets for sustainability. These experiences collectively shaped my understanding of self-management. I moved from viewing self-management as willpower and discipline ("try harder") to viewing it as systems design and honest data ("build systems that make success easy"). This shift is fundamental to sustaining the performance required for 500-partnership scaling.

## Progression through Practice

I progressed in self-management through hands-on practice in four areas: systematic habit tracking, morning routine design, capacity planning, and honest target setting.

**Systematic Habit Tracking (October 2025 onwards):** This involved launching and using the time-tracking app (Objective 2, 85% progress). The app includes nightly checkout verification: cannabis-free checkbox (yes/no with optional written justification), focused hours logged (automated aggregation), work type categorization (deep work vs admin vs learning). The key practice was honest self-tracking—not gaming the system, not fudging the numbers. The ~50% actual compliance data was uncomfortable to confront but essential for realistic target setting. This practice taught me that self-awareness requires data, not perception. The app successfully supported capacity planning for the 100-partner milestone (achieved April 2026).

**Morning Routine Design (September 2025 - January 2026):** This involved establishing the 6:30 AM wake routine. The progression was: September (inconsistent) → October (commitment to 6:30 AM regardless of previous night) → November-December (building the sequence: meditation → exercise → planning → deep work start) → January 2026 onwards (80%+ consistency measured in app). The key learning was that consistency matters more than perfection. 80% consistency on the morning routine creates most of the value. The routine creates the foundation for the 50 focused hours/week target—starting the day proactively rather than reactively.

**Capacity Planning (October 2025 - December 2025):** This involved determining sustainable work capacity. The original target was 70 focused hours/week. I attempted this for 6-8 weeks, tracking energy levels and output quality. I recognised early burnout signals: diminishing returns, fatigue, reduced decision quality. The insight was that sustainable capacity is lower than maximum capacity. I adjusted the target to 50 focused hours/week. This isn't about limiting ambition—it's about sustainability. The 100-partnership achievement was accomplished with this sustainable approach, validating that quality focused hours matter more than total hours.

**Honest Target Setting (November 2025 - January 2026):** This involved adjusting targets based on evidence rather than ego. The cannabis-free target shifted from 100% to 80% (24+ days/month). The focused hours target shifted from 70 to 50 per week. These adjustments weren't about lowering standards—they were about aligning targets with reality. The 40% progress rating on Objective 1 reflects the gap between current performance (~50% actual compliance) and target (80%). This honest assessment is valuable because it provides accurate baseline for improvement. The alternative would be claiming 100% compliance while missing the target—this would feel better in the short term but prevent real improvement.

Putting self-management into practice required confronting uncomfortable truths about my actual habits and capacity. The time-tracking app data didn't lie. I could choose to ignore it (maintain the illusion of perfect habits) or use it (confront reality and improve). I chose the latter. This commitment to truth-telling, even when it reveals gaps, is essential for growth.

Practicing this competency contributed to my overall development by teaching me that sustainable high performance requires systematic self-management, not just episodic bursts of effort. The progression from Year 2 (smoking cessation through determination) to Year 3 (systematic habit management through data and systems) represents the shift from heroic effort to sustainable practice.

## Progression through Enquiry

Enquiry-based learning contributed to my self-management development, particularly through understanding the science of habit formation and capacity management.

Key frameworks studied:

**Atomic Habits (James Clear):** I learned that 1% better every day equals 37x improvement in a year. The key insight is that habits form through consistency, not intensity. I aimed for 100% compliance initially, but the reality was 80%+ consistency. The learning was that 80% consistency creates most of the value—perfection isn't required. I also learned that habits stack—attaching new habits to existing routines makes them more likely to stick. The 6:30 AM routine works because it's anchored to sleep and preceded by evening preparation.

**Circadian Rhythms Research:** I studied sleep science and productivity research to understand energy management. The insight was that energy levels follow predictable patterns throughout the day. Deep work is best during peak energy hours (morning for most people). Sleep consistency is foundational to all performance. This influenced my 6:30 AM wake routine (aligning with natural circadian rhythm) and scheduling deep work in morning hours. The morning routine (6:30 AM → meditation → exercise → planning → deep work start) leverages peak energy hours for strategic work.

**Deep Work and Flow State Research (Cal Newport):** I learned that focused work is rare and valuable in the attention economy. Flow state requires specific conditions: challenge + skill balance, clear goals, immediate feedback. The insight was that 50 focused hours/week of deep work is more valuable than 70 hours of shallow work. This influenced my capacity planning—prioritising deep work (partnerships, clients, strategy) over shallow work (email, Slack, admin). The time-tracking app categorizes work types to enable this analysis.

**Burnout Prevention Research:** I studied athlete performance literature and burnout research to understand sustainable capacity. The key insight was that burnout is preventable, not inevitable. Sustainable capacity is lower than maximum capacity. Recovery (sleep, exercise, rest) is as important as work. This influenced my decision to adjust from 70 to 50 focused hours/week. The goal is long-term sustainable performance, not short-term maximum exertion.

**Habit Formation Science:** I researched how long it takes to form habits (21-66 days average, varies by person) and what makes habits stick. The insight was that missing one day doesn't break habit, but consistency over time matters. I aimed for 100% but achieved 80%+ on the morning routine. The learning was that 80% consistency is sufficient for most benefits—perfectionism can be counterproductive if it leads to abandoning the routine entirely.

How this influenced my professional values and practices:

Self-management research reinforced my commitment to sustainability over intensity. The old approach was maximum effort, burnout, recover, repeat. The new approach is sustainable capacity, consistent habits, long-term performance. This values shift is evident in the target adjustments: 80% cannabis-free (sustainable during scaling) vs 100% (unrealistic), 50 focused hours/week (sustainable) vs 70 (maximum but not sustainable).

I also developed a stronger appreciation for systems over willpower. The time-tracking app, morning routine, and honest target setting are all systems that make success easier. Willpower is a finite resource—systems are reliable. The progression from Year 2 (willpower-based smoking cessation) to Year 3 (systems-based habit management) represents this learning.

By integrating this knowledge, I've been able to build self-management systems that support rather than hinder my goals. The time-tracking app (85% progress, Objective 2) provides data for honest self-assessment. The 6:30 AM routine creates daily foundation. The adjusted targets (80%, 50 hours) align ambition with sustainability. These systems enabled the capacity required for the 100-partnership achievement (April 2026).

However, I recognise that there's still a gap between target and performance. The 40% progress on Objective 1 reflects that actual compliance (~50%) is below target (80%). Closing this gap requires continued focus on self-management systems and practice.

## Key Learning

One significant learning moment occurred during the Delegation to Sam undertaking (December 2025 - January 2026). I had implemented a daily task tracking system with £2.49/day fines for missed tracking, believing that financial accountability would drive compliance. After 60 days, both Sam and I owed hundreds of pounds in fines, so we wrote off the system. The profound insight was: "When systems fail, look at system design before blaming people." Sam successfully completed all the infrastructure and client acquisition work he was assigned—I never had to redo anything. The problem wasn't Sam's capability or motivation; it was my system design. I had created a tracking system that was burdensome, then blamed people for not using it. This taught me that leadership is about designing systems that make the right work easy, not just imposing consequences for the wrong work.

Another key learning came from researching strategic communication and influence (LfO-04, October-December 2025). My analysis revealed that only 40% of my Team Apollo recommendations were being adopted, despite being technically sound. The research—spanning Cialdini's "Influence," "Crucial Conversations," and HBR stakeholder management articles—taught me that adoption is not about the quality of your solution but about the quality of your communication process. I was using logic when humans decide based on psychology. I was presenting solutions without building buy-in first. I was framing solutions in my language, not theirs. This insight fundamentally changed my leadership approach: I now start with questions ("What are you seeing? What concerns do you have?") rather than answers ("Here's the solution").

A third insight emerged from the hiring process for the first partnership manager (targeted for January 2026). The partnership revenue generated from the 100-partner target gives financial capacity to hire, but I realised that hiring before having documented systems would be irresponsible. The LbD-04 experience had taught me that Sam's capability wasn't the bottleneck—my systems were. This led to the recognition that strategic leadership requires designing the organisational systems before scaling headcount. The objective targets hiring 2 partnership managers by June 2026, but only after processes are documented.

These experiences collectively shaped my understanding of strategic leadership. I moved from viewing leadership as "directing people to do work" to viewing it as "designing systems that enable others to succeed." This shift is fundamental to scaling from 100 to 500 partnerships. The progression from Year 2 (struggling with delegation) to Year 3 (systematically building team capability) represents significant development, but I recognise that I'm still early in the journey. The true test will be whether the systems I'm building can enable a team of three partnership managers to operate without my daily involvement.

I progressed in strategic leadership through hands-on practice in three areas: team development, organisational design, and stakeholder influence.

Phase 2 (November-December) involved guided independence—Sam managing partnerships with weekly review sessions. The key learning was that development follows a clear trajectory: direct supervision → guided practice → independent execution. However, the failed tracking system experiment (LbD-04) taught me that delegation requires systems that are easy to use, not just systems that are comprehensive. Sam successfully completed all assigned work (I never had to redo anything), proving capability isn't the bottleneck—system design is.

**Organisational Design (January 2026 onwards):** The first partnership manager hire (targeted January 2026, 25% progress achieved) marks the transition from individual contributor to organisational builder. I documented partnership processes in the partnership app: onboarding checklists, outreach email templates, client communication scripts. The goal is to create organisational memory that doesn't depend on me. This practice taught me that organisations scale through systems, not individual effort. The 25% progress rating on Objective 3 reflects both progress (first hire made, Sam at 12+ partnerships) and the long journey ahead (need to hire 2nd partnership manager, Sam needs to reach 50+ partnerships, all processes need documentation).

**Stakeholder Influence (October 2025 - December 2025):** The LfO-04 undertaking on Strategic Communication & Influence was a practice in influence without authority. The 40% adoption rate of my Team Apollo recommendations revealed that I was providing technical direction without ensuring adoption. The research and application changed my approach: (1) Ask questions before giving answers, (2) Frame solutions in terms of the other person's interests, not mine, (3) Use stories and concrete examples, not just abstract logic. I applied Cialdini's principles of influence (social proof, commitment, reciprocity) to partnership conversations. Early data shows improved adoption, though I'm still tracking results.

Putting strategic leadership into practice required confronting my own control tendencies. The LbD-04 experience was painful but necessary—I had to acknowledge that the tracking system failure was my leadership failure, not Sam's performance failure. I explicitly told Sam this, which built trust. Vulnerability and admitting mistakes are not weaknesses in leadership—they're essential for building psychological safety.

Practicing this competency contributed to my overall development by forcing me to confront the difference between individual contribution and organisational impact. In Year 2, I measured my contribution by what I personally accomplished. In Year 3, I'm learning to measure my contribution by what the team accomplishes. This represents a fundamental identity shift that's essential for scaling.

## Progression through Enquiry

Enquiry-based learning was critical for developing my strategic leadership capability. I engaged with frameworks, research, and case studies that shaped my approach to building teams and influencing stakeholders.

Key frameworks studied:

**Hersey-Blanchard Situational Leadership Model:** This framework from Year 2 continued to inform my approach in Year 3. The model argues that leadership style should match follower readiness: Directing (for beginners) → Coaching (for learners) → Supporting (for developing performers) → Delegating (for experts). I applied this systematically to Sam's development: Phase 1 was Directing (Sam shadowing me), Phase 2 was Coaching (Sam managing partnerships with guidance), Phase 3 was Supporting (Sam managing independently with check-ins). The goal is Phase 4 (Delegating) where Sam can mentor new hires. This framework provided the roadmap for Sam's progression.

**Cialdini's Six Principles of Influence:** From "Influence: The Psychology of Persuasion," I learned that influence is based on psychology, not logic. The six principles—Reciprocity, Commitment/Consistency, Social Proof, Authority, Liking, Scarcity—explain why recommendations get adopted or ignored. My 40% adoption problem was because I was using logic when humans decide based on psychology. I now apply these principles systematically to partnership conversations: social proof, commitment (small pilots first), reciprocity (give value first), and authority (reference case studies).

**Crucial Conversations Framework:** This book taught me that when stakes are high and opinions differ, humans either go silent or violent. The solution is "Start with Heart"—know what you really want, then make it safe for others to share their views. My 40% adoption problem was likely because I was making conversations unsafe by coming in with "the answer." I changed my approach to start with questions: "What are you seeing? What concerns do you have?" This makes it safe for others to participate in the solution.

**Stakeholder Adoption Curve:** From HBR stakeholder management articles, I learned that adoption follows a predictable curve: Unaware → Resistant → Neutral → Supportive → Committed. The mistake is presenting solutions to unaware or resistant stakeholders and expecting immediate adoption. I now diagnose where stakeholders are and tailor communication accordingly.

Key insights from different sources:

**From "High Output Management" by Andy Grove:** I learned that accountability systems should enable performance, not create bureaucracy. The tracking system failure (LbD-04) violated this principle.

**From SaaS leadership case studies:** I learned that companies successfully scale from founder-led to team-led by (1) documenting all processes, (2) hiring for culture fit and capability, (3) investing in training and development, and (4) creating clear career progression.

How this influenced my professional values and practices:

Strategic leadership research reinforced my commitment to empowerment over control. The tracking system failure taught me that control-based leadership doesn't work. Empowerment-based leadership (clear processes, easy systems, natural accountability) does.

I also developed a stronger appreciation for systems thinking in leadership. Great leadership isn't about being charismatic or inspirational—it's about designing organisational systems that produce results.

By integrating this knowledge, I've been able to make progress in team development despite the challenges. Sam's progression from 0 to 12+ partnerships, the first hire, and the basic process documentation all represent application of these leadership frameworks. However, I recognise that I'm still early in the journey. The true test will be whether these systems enable a team of three to manage 500 partnerships without my daily involvement.

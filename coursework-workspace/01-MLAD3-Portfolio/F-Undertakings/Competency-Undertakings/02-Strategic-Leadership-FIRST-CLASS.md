## Competency

**What is the name and definition of this competency?**

Strategic Leadership: The ability to envision and communicate a compelling future, build and develop high-performing teams, and create systems that enable scalable organisational success through others rather than individual effort.

**How is this competency relevant to my situation and SMART Objectives?**

Strategic leadership is critical because scaling from individual effort to team-based delivery requires learning to lead through systems, not just direct involvement. The evidence of my leadership development is a documented failure that produced profound learning.

From December 2025 to January 2026, I attempted to delegate infrastructure setup and e-commerce client acquisition to Sam. I implemented a tracking system with £2.49/day fines for missed task tracking. After 60 days, both of us owed hundreds of pounds in fines, so we wrote off the system. This leadership failure taught me a critical lesson: "When systems fail, look at system design before blaming people." Sam successfully completed all assigned work - I never had to redo anything. The problem wasn't Sam's capability; it was my burdensome tracking system.

This experience demonstrates strategic leadership through vulnerability: acknowledging my design failure, learning that good systems make the right work easy (not just track wrong work), and recognising that Sam's capability validates the delegation strategy even if my first system failed.

SMART Objective 3 targets team-based delivery. The delegation attempt was the first step toward that goal, and the failure provided the systems-thinking foundation required for successful scaling.

## Key Learning

One significant learning moment is documented in LbD-04-Delegation-to-Sam.md (12,473 bytes). I attempted to delegate infrastructure setup and client acquisition to Sam, implementing a daily task tracking system with £2.49/day fines for missed tracking. After 60 days, both Sam and I owed hundreds of pounds in fines, so we wrote off the system.

The profound insight: **"When systems fail, look at system design before blaming people."**

Sam successfully completed all the infrastructure and client acquisition work successfully - I never had to redo anything. The problem wasn't Sam's capability or motivation - it was my system design. I created a tracking system that was burdensome, and then blamed people for not using it.

Another key learning: **"Extrinsic motivation (financial penalties) doesn't work when the process itself is difficult. Good systems make the right behavior easy, not just punished when wrong."**

The tracking system failed because it was easier to skip tracking than to do it daily. The £2.49 penalty was meant to incentivise tracking, but the real problem was that the tracking system itself was too cumbersome. I should have designed a system that was easy to use, not one with penalties for non-use.

A third insight: **"Never having to redo Sam's work proves he's capable. The bottleneck is my system design, not his performance."**

This validated my decision to delegate to Sam. He successfully completed infrastructure setup and client acquisition work without rework. The failure was entirely in the tracking system, not the operational work. This means Sam can handle responsibility if I provide better processes.

The vulnerability of acknowledging this failure is itself evidence of leadership growth. Leaders who can't admit mistakes don't learn from them. I told Sam explicitly that the tracking system was my mistake, not his - this honesty builds trust and shows I take responsibility for system design failures.

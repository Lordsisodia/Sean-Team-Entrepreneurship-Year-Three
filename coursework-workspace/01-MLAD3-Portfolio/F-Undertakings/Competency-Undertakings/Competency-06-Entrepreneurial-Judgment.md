- Specific decision-making framework implementation
- Strategic review processes
- Mentorship on prioritization

```
[Your response here]

Example: I implemented three structured decision-making practices: (1) Weekly strategic
reviews evaluating all activities against the 1,000 partnership goal, (2) Opportunity Cost
Analysis before committing significant time to any initiative, and (3) Pre-Mortem scenario
planning for major strategic decisions.

For weekly strategic reviews, I allocated Friday afternoons to assess: "What did I work on
this week?" "Did it advance the partnership goal?" "What should I stop, start, or continue?"
This systematic reflection revealed I was spending 60% of time on low-leverage activities:
app refinement, technical exploration, reactive requests rather than strategic priorities.

For Opportunity Cost Analysis, I implemented a simple framework: before committing to any
project longer than 1 week, ask: "What am I NOT doing by choosing this?" "What's the
expected ROI of this vs. alternatives?" "How does this connect to the 9-month AI window
urgency?" This prevented repeating the internal app mistake—I now explicitly evaluate trade-offs
rather than defaulting to what feels comfortable.

For Pre-Mortem planning, I applied it to the partnership scaling strategy: "Assume we fail to
reach 100 partnerships by January. Why would that happen?" This surfaced risks I'd missed:
insufficient demand validation, unclear value proposition, inadequate sales processes, Sam's
bandwidth constraints. I built mitigation plans for each, making the strategy more robust.

I also sought mentorship from successful entrepreneurs on resource allocation under
uncertainty. Key advice: "Revenue now vs. systems later is a false dichotomy—build systems
THAT generate revenue, not systems FOR future revenue." This reshaped my approach to app
development prioritization.
```

### Critical Evaluation (200-250 words)
- What judgment improvements did you achieve?
- What challenges did you face in strategic decision-making?
- What would you do differently?
- Honest assessment of your prioritization failures

```
[Your response here]

Example: The hardest part was admitting the 6-week internal app investment wasn't just a
wrong choice—it was a judgment failure pattern. I've made similar mistakes before: choosing
comfortable technical work over uncomfortable strategic outreach, prioritizing perfect systems
over imperfect revenue generation, favoring immediate progress over long-term leverage.
Entrepreneurial Judgment requires recognizing these patterns and overriding the impulse to
retreat into what I know.

I struggled with Opportunity Cost Analysis in practice. The concept is simple, but the
emotional reality is difficult. Saying "no" to interesting technical explorations,
potential client projects, or networking opportunities feels like missing out. I found myself
rationalizing exceptions: "This could be valuable," "This won't take much time," "This is
different." Each exception eroded strategic focus. I estimated achieving only ~50% adherence
to opportunity cost discipline initially.

The most surprising insight: uncertainty triggers my comfort-seeking bias. When outcomes feel
predictable (app development, technical systems, client projects with clear specs), I'm
confident making decisions. When outcomes feel uncertain (partnership market response,
revenue model validation, scaling challenges), I default to familiar technical work.
Entrepreneurial Judgment means becoming comfortable making decisions with incomplete
information—and accepting that some decisions will be wrong.

What I'd do differently: Apply decision frameworks earlier, before committing time. The internal
app mistake wouldn't have happened with systematic opportunity cost analysis—asking "What's
the ROI of internal tools vs. partnership apps?" would have revealed the strategic misalignment.
Also, I'd involve others in major decisions—Sam, my TE coach, mentors—to reduce individual
blind spots and biases. Solo decision-making under uncertainty is高风险; structured input
from others improves judgment quality.
```

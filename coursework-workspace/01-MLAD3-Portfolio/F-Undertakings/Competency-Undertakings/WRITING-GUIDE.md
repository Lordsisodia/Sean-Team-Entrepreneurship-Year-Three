## 📝 COMPETENCY UNDERTAKING STRUCTURE

Each undertaking should be **500-600 words total** with these sections:

### **1. Context (50-75 words)**
- Which competency this addresses
- Current level vs. target level
- Why this competency matters for your exit strategy
- Connect to Year 2 baseline

**Example template:**
> "In Year 2, I identified [competency] as a challenge: [Year 2 evidence]. For Year 3, this competency is critical because [connection to SMART objective/partnership scaling]. I aimed to develop from [current level] to [target level], measured by [success metric]."

---

### **2. Action Taken (150-200 words)**
- **Specific activities** undertaken (not vague "I worked on it")
- **Timeline** - when did this happen? (October 2025 - January 2026)
- **Resources used** - mentorship, courses, reading, practice
- **People involved** - Sam, team members, TE coach, mentors
- **Frameworks applied** - specific models/theories

**Example template:**
> "I developed [competency] through [specific activities]: (1) [Activity 1 with details], (2) [Activity 2 with details], (3) [Activity 3 with details].
>
> For [activity 1], I [specific action with timeline]. This involved [people/frameworks]. The outcome was [specific result].
>
> I also utilized [resource/course/mentorship]. Key learning from [resource]: [specific insight applied].
>
> [Activity 2] focused on [specific aspect]. Timeline: [when]. This connected to my SMART objective by [connection]."

---

### **3. Critical Evaluation (200-250 words)**
This is the **most important section** for Outstanding quality. Must include:

- **What challenged your assumptions?** (not just "what went well")
- **What surprised you?** (unexpected insights)
- **What mistakes did you make?** (honest failure discussion)
- **What would you do differently?** (genuine reflection)
- **How did this change your understanding?** (depth of learning)

**Example template:**
> "The hardest part was [specific challenge]. I expected [expectation] but [reality]. This challenged my assumption that [assumption].
>
> I struggled with [specific difficulty]. For example: [concrete example]. I found myself [pattern of behavior], which reduced effectiveness.
>
> The most surprising insight: [unexpected learning]. This contradicted my belief that [previous belief]. The data showed [evidence that surprised you].
>
> What I'd do differently: [specific changes]. If I were starting again, I would [different approach]. Also, I would [additional improvement].
>
> This connects to Year 2 challenges: [reference to Year 2 struggle]. The difference this time: [what changed]."

---

### **4. Learning Achieved (100-125 words)**
- **Specific skills** developed (not vague "I improved")
- **Measurable progress** - what improved? (with numbers)
- **Connection to objectives** - which SMART objective does this support?
- **Impact** - what changed in ventures/projects/team?

**Example template:**
> "I developed [specific skills]: [skill 1], [skill 2], [skill 3]. More importantly, I learned that [key insight].
>
> This connects directly to [SMART Objective]: [connection explanation]. The measurable impact: [specific numbers/outcomes].
>
> [Before/after comparison]: Before developing this competency, [situation]. After: [improved situation with metrics].
>
> This progress demonstrates [competency] development because [evidence of growth]."

---

### **5. Future Application (50-75 words)**
- **Continued development** - what needs more work?
- **Next milestone** - what's the specific next step?
- **Long-term application** - how will this support post-TE goals?

**Example template:**
> "Continue developing [competency] by [specific next steps]. Target [next milestone] by [timeline].
>
> For scaling to 500 partnerships, this competency will enable [specific application]. Post-TE, [long-term use].
>
> Remaining gaps: [honest assessment of what still needs work]. Next focus: [specific area]."

## 🎨 OUTSTANDING QUALITY INDICATORS

### ✅ **DO This:**

**Content Quality:**
- Honest assessment of current level (not overstating)
- Specific development activities with dates, names, details
- Critical evaluation depth (challenges, surprises, mistakes)
- Concrete application examples with measurable impact
- Valid evidence of development (numbers, outcomes, testimonials)

**Depth of Reflection:**
- Connections to Year 2 Learning Contract
- Links to SMART Objectives (explicit references)
- Integration of theory/frameworks (Hersey-Blanchard, Gibb's, etc.)
- Awareness of limitations and remaining gaps
- Clear next steps for continued development

**Writing Quality:**
- Clear, professional language
- Appropriate word count (~500-600 each)
- Logical structure and flow
- Specific examples and details
- No fluff or filler

### ❌ **DON'T Do This:**

- Vague statements like "I improved my communication"
- Ignoring failures and only mentioning successes
- Overstating current skill level
- Disconnected from actual experiences
- No specific examples or evidence
- Superficial reflection without depth

## 📊 WRITING TEMPLATES FOR EACH COMPETENCY

### **Template 1: Strategic Leadership**

```
Context (75 words):
In Year 2, I identified delegation as a critical leadership challenge (Learning Contract: "Where have I been?"). For Year 3, Strategic Leadership is essential to scale from 100→500 partnerships—I cannot be the bottleneck. My baseline: Sam managing 12+ partnerships (January 2026). Target: Sam managing 50+ by August 2026, enabling me to focus on strategic scaling while team handles operations.

Action Taken (200 words):
I developed Strategic Leadership through systematic delegation and team development: (1) Weekly delegation sessions with Sam, (2) Process documentation for all partnership activities, (3) Hiring and onboarding first partnership manager, (4) Leadership framework research (LfO-04 Strategic Communication).

Starting October 2025, I implemented weekly 1-hour sessions with Sam focused on partnership handovers. Each session: "Which partnerships need my input?" "What can you handle independently?" "What support do you need?" This shifted from reactive delegation to proactive capacity building.

I documented 100% of partnership processes: 15-step onboarding checklist, 3 email sequence templates, client communication scripts, weekly reporting framework, escalation protocols. This documentation (60+ pages) enables scalable training—new hires can self-onboard using these systems.

In January 2026, I hired the first partnership manager (funded by £50K monthly revenue from 100 partners). The onboarding process used documented systems, reducing training time by 70% compared to Sam's ad-hoc training.

I also researched leadership frameworks through LfO-04 Strategic Communication & Influence undertaking. Key insights: delegating outcomes vs. tasks, building capability not just capacity, creating systems that scale beyond individuals.

Critical Evaluation (250 words):
The hardest part was resisting the urge to step back in when Sam struggled with partnership management. My INTJ temperament's need for control (Year 2 assessment) made delegation uncomfortable. When Sam mishandled a partner communication in November, I almost intervened—but stopped myself. Sam resolved it independently, learning more from the experience than if I had stepped in. This pattern appeared repeatedly: I had to override my instinct to "fix it myself" and let Sam learn through practice.

I struggled with finding the right delegation balance. Initially, I delegated too little—only low-risk tasks—which limited Sam's growth and my strategic focus. Then I swung too far—delegating critical partnerships without sufficient support—which created stress. Finding the middle ground: delegate with structured support (clear processes, escalation protocols, weekly check-ins) took 3 months of iteration.

The most surprising insight: documentation IS delegation. I used to think delegation meant "telling someone what to do." I learned that effective delegation means "building systems that enable others to succeed independently." The 60+ pages of process documentation were more valuable to Sam's development than any conversation—documented systems reduced ambiguity, built confidence, and enabled autonomous decision-making.

What I'd do differently: Start delegation earlier. I lost October-December 2025 trying to do everything myself before implementing systematic delegation. Also, I'd involve Sam in designing the delegation system—we built processes FOR Sam, not WITH Sam, which reduced ownership and required more iteration. Co-creating delegation systems would have accelerated Sam's capacity building.

This connects to Year 2's leadership challenge: I identified delegation struggles but didn't systematically address them. Year 3's approach (weekly sessions + documentation + hiring) is structured, scalable, and measurable—a significant improvement over Year 2's ad-hoc delegation attempts.

Learning Achieved (125 words):
I developed specific Strategic Leadership skills: systematic delegation (outcome-focused, not task-focused), process documentation (60+ pages enabling scalable training), team development (Sam 0→12+ partnerships), and leadership framework application (LfO-04 research integrated).

More importantly, I learned that leadership at scale requires building systems, not just directing people. The shift from "how do I get Sam to do this?" to "how do I build systems that enable Sam (and others) to succeed independently?" fundamentally changed my approach.

This connects directly to SMART Objective 3 (Team Capacity Building): Sam's progression from 0→12+ partnerships (October 2025-January 2026) demonstrates leadership development. Target: 50+ partnerships by August 2026. The systems we've built (documentation, escalation protocols, weekly reviews) enable this scaling.

Future Application (75 words):
Continue refining delegation systems based on real-world effectiveness. Target: Sam managing 50+ partnerships by August 2026, then hire 2 additional partnership managers using the same systematic approach.

For 500-partner scaling, Strategic Leadership will require building team capability at scale—not just developing Sam, but creating leadership development systems that work for any team member. Post-TE, this approach will apply to any venture: systems-first leadership scales; hero-founder leadership doesn't.
```

### **Template 2: Professional Communication**

```
Context (75 words):
In Year 2, I identified communication challenges: providing technical direction without ensuring adoption (Learning Contract: "Challenges"). For Year 3, Professional Communication is critical for partnership scaling—I must influence 100+ agency partners, not just inform them. Baseline: manual outreach at 60 conversations/week with ~10% conversion. Target: automated system at 75 conversations/week with 15% conversion through strategic communication.

Action Taken (200 words):
I developed Professional Communication through systematic outreach optimization: (1) A/B testing messaging frameworks, (2) Partner communication playbook development, (3) Community building (Slack, webinars), (4) Communication research (influence, stakeholder management).

Starting October 2025, I implemented A/B testing on all partnership outreach. For each campaign, I tested 3 message variations: (A) feature-focused (our technology), (B) outcome-focused (client results), (C) partnership-focused (revenue share). Results: outcome-focused messages achieved 12-15% conversion vs. 8-10% for feature-focused. This data drove systematic message refinement.

I documented all communication patterns in a partnership playbook: cold outreach templates (3-touch cadence), follow-up sequences, objection handling scripts, closing frameworks, partner update emails. This playbook (40+ templates) enabled consistent, high-quality communication across 100+ partnerships.

For community building, I launched a private Slack community (80+ active partners) and monthly training webinars (40-60 attendees). Communication shifted from 1:1 outreach to 1:many influence—webinars scale communication efficiency 10x vs. individual calls.

I also researched influence frameworks through LfO undertakings. Key insights: outcome-focused messaging beats feature-focused, social proof (partner testimonials) outperforms claims, exclusivity (limited partnerships) increased urgency.

Critical Evaluation (250 words):
The hardest part was shifting from technical to strategic communication. My INTJ profile (Year 2) prefers logical, detailed explanations—but partnership prospects responded to concise, outcome-focused messaging. I had to suppress my instinct to explain "how it works" and focus on "what you'll achieve." This felt like oversimplification, but conversion data proved it was more effective.

I struggled with message personalization at scale. With 100+ partners, 1:1 personalized communication became impossible. I found myself defaulting to generic templates—which reduced engagement. The solution: semi-personalization (template + 2-3 customized sentences per prospect) achieved 90% of personalization benefits with 20% of the effort. Finding this balance required testing 20+ variations over 3 months.

The most surprising insight: community communication outperforms direct outreach. The Slack community (80+ partners) generated 30% of new partnership leads through peer-to-peer referrals. Partners selling to other partners was 3x more effective than me selling directly. This contradicted my belief that direct 1:1 communication was most effective. The data showed that building communication channels for others to communicate scales better than being the primary communicator.

What I'd do differently: start community building earlier. I focused on 1:1 outreach October-December 2025 before launching the Slack community (January 2026). Lost opportunity: community-driven referrals could have accelerated the 100-partner achievement. Also, I'd involve partners in message development—I wrote all templates internally, but partner feedback on messaging would have improved conversion faster.

This connects to Year 2's communication challenge: I provided technical direction but didn't ensure adoption. Year 3's approach (outcome-focused messaging, community building, A/B testing) measures effectiveness through conversion data, not just whether information was delivered.

Learning Achieved (125 words):
I developed specific Professional Communication skills: outcome-focused messaging (15% higher conversion), A/B testing frameworks, community building (80+ partners in Slack), and scalable communication systems (playbooks, templates, webinars).

More importantly, I learned that influence at scale requires strategic communication, not just information delivery. The shift from "what do I want to say?" to "what will resonate with prospects?" fundamentally changed my approach. Communication effectiveness is measured by action (partnerships signed), not understanding (information delivered).

This connects directly to SMART Objective 4 (Advanced Automation): 60→75 conversations/week with 10%→15% conversion through strategic communication optimization. The playbook and community systems enable this scaling.

Future Application (75 words):
Continue refining communication frameworks based on conversion data. Target: 75 conversations/week at 15% conversion by June 2026, then scale to 500 partners using the same systems.

For 500-partner scaling, Professional Communication will require automated personalization, community-led growth, and data-driven message optimization. Post-TE, these communication principles apply to any venture: measure influence by outcomes, not outputs.
```

## Competency

**What is the name and definition of this competency?**

Professional Communication: The ability to convey ideas effectively, influence stakeholders through strategic messaging, and create scalable communication systems that drive organisational success.

**How is this competency relevant to my situation and SMART Objectives?**

Professional communication is fundamental to partnership scaling. The evidence of my communication development is a specific problem identified and systematically addressed: I discovered that only 40% of my Team Apollo recommendations were being adopted despite technical soundness.

Through systematic research (LfO-04: 18,652 bytes), I identified the root causes: I was presenting solutions without buy-in, using logic when psychology drives decisions, framing in my language not theirs, and triggering identity threats. I applied the "Start with Heart" framework from *Crucial Conversations*, shifting from "Here's the solution" to asking "What are you seeing? What concerns do you have?"

The e-commerce client work (LbD-01) demonstrates communication in practice: 7-8 rounds of iteration where I implemented all requested changes without resistance, learning that the client knows their business better than I do. The difficult crypto client experience (LbD-05) taught me client communication boundaries - when to end relationships that don't respect professional communication standards. The construction deal collapse (LbD-06) taught resilience in handling ambiguous "no" responses and continuing outreach despite rejection.

SMART Objective 6 targets 100 agency partnerships. Communication skill is the primary driver of partnership acquisition - the ability to build rapport, ensure adoption, and maintain relationships through uncertainty.

## Progression Summary

### Three-Year Communication Trajectory

**Year 1 (2023-24): Theoretical Foundation and Core Techniques**

At the start of Year 1, I engaged in systematic study of communication principles through multiple LfO undertakings. The **"Think Fast, Talk Smart"** workshop (April 2024) provided foundational communication techniques: active listening, audience adaptation, message structuring, and handling difficult conversations. This was complemented by **"The 7 Habits of Highly Effective People"** study, which introduced Habit 5: "Seek First to Understand, Then to Be Understood" - the principle of empathetic listening as the foundation of effective communication.

The Year 1 learning established my baseline understanding: communication is two-way, listening precedes speaking, and adaptation to audience is crucial. However, this was primarily theoretical knowledge without systematic application framework.

**Year 2 (2024-25): Application and Testing**

Year 2 provided my first significant communication challenges in real-world contexts. The **AI Influencer scaling project** (Agency App Marketing Automation LbD) required developing content strategy and messaging to attract clients. I crafted tailored value propositions and learned to adapt communication to different audience segments - early practice in audience awareness.

The **SISO Partnership Program** (LbD) involved identifying partners, outlining value propositions, and conducting outreach. I learned that partnership communication requires balancing assertiveness with empathy, and that tailored messaging is more effective than generic pitches. However, I was still operating primarily from a "technical solution first" mindset - focusing on what I offered rather than what partners needed.

During team presentations and strategic initiatives, I practiced verbal communication and persuasion. The Year 2 Communication Skills competency reflection noted progress toward "advanced level" through content development, public speaking, and team collaboration. I was improving, but the gap between "providing direction" and "influencing stakeholders" had not yet been identified.

**Year 3 (2025-26): Mastery Through Systematic Problem-Solving**

Year 3 began with my assessment at Advanced level. My Year 2 baseline included +25% improvement in client satisfaction, but the **40% adoption rate discovery** revealed a critical gap that transformed my understanding of professional communication.

Unlike Years 1 and 2 where I learned communication techniques, Year 3 was defined by **systematic problem-solving** through LfO-04 (October-December 2025). I researched why smart recommendations get ignored, studying Cialdini's influence principles, *Crucial Conversations*, *Made to Stick*, and HBR stakeholder management. This wasn't learning new techniques - it was discovering that **adoption is not about solution quality, but communication process quality**.

The key shift: from "transferring information" to "ensuring adoption through psychology and systematic process." I changed from "analyze problem → develop solution → present recommendation" to "ask questions → understand perspective → co-create solution → build buy-in → check for commitment."

The e-commerce client work (LbD-01: 7-8 iteration rounds), difficult client management (LbD-05), and construction deal resilience (LbD-06) provided practical testing ground for new communication patterns. The learning that "client knows their business better than I do" and "professional communication includes boundaries" represents maturity that goes beyond technique to judgment.

### Assessment Accuracy

My initial assessment was accurate in identifying the adoption gap, but I underestimated how fundamental psychology is to communication. The research revealed that humans don't adopt based on logic - they adopt based on emotion, identity, social proof, and psychological safety. This insight, missing from Years 1 and 2, transformed Year 3 communication from technique to behavioral change.

Key progression factors: (1) Systematic research through LfO-04, (2) Honest assessment of 40% adoption problem, (3) Applied framework change (questions before answers), (4) Client communication experiences providing practical learning ground, (5) Three-year foundation enabling systematic problem identification and solution.

### Year 3 Key Learning Moments

**One: The 40% Adoption Problem and Systematic Research**

Through LfO-04 research, I identified four mistakes I was making:

1. **Presenting solutions without buy-in first** - I violated "Start with Heart" from *Crucial Conversations*
2. **Using logic when psychology drives decisions** - I ignored Cialdini's principles
3. **Framing in my language, not theirs** - I triggered identity threats
4. **Assuming technical quality ensures adoption** - I ignored communication process quality

The profound insight: **"Adoption is not about the quality of your solution - it's about the quality of your communication process."**

This learning connects directly back to Year 1's "Seek First to Understand" principle, but operationalizes it through specific frameworks. Year 1 taught me to listen; Year 3 taught me **how to structure communication for adoption**.

**Two: E-commerce Client Iteration and Communication Maturity**

The e-commerce client work (LbD-01) went through 7-8 rounds of iteration. I implemented all requested changes without resistance. The learning: **"Communication maturity is recognising that the client knows their business better than you do. Your job is to enable their success, not defend your technical preferences."**

This represents evolution from Year 2's content strategy work, where I focused on crafting persuasive messages. Year 3 taught me that persuasion isn't about winning arguments - it's about enabling client success through iterative co-creation.

**Three: Difficult Client Management and Communication Boundaries**

The difficult crypto client experience (LbD-05) taught: **"Professional communication includes boundaries. Some clients don't respect professional communication standards, and continuing those relationships damages both parties."**

Client selection became part of my communication strategy: qualify clients before committing to projects. The qualification framework asks: Do they respect professional boundaries? Are they reasonable about timelines? Do I feel energised or drained after talking to them?

This learning - that communication includes knowing when to **end** conversations - represents maturity beyond technique to judgment.

**Four: Framework Change - Questions Before Answers**

I changed my approach from "analyze problem → develop solution → present recommendation" to:
1. Ask questions first
2. Understand perspective
3. Co-create solution
4. Build buy-in
5. Check for commitment

This framework change is already showing improved adoption - stakeholders now participate in solution design rather than resisting recommendations.

The "questions before answers" pattern connects to Year 1's "Think Fast, Talk Smart" workshop, but Year 3 operationalizes it through specific stakeholder management processes rather than general communication awareness.

### Year 3 Enquiry: Systematic Research and Framework Development

**LfO-04: Strategic Communication & Influence** (18,652 bytes, October-December 2025):

**Core Sources Studied:**

1. **"Influence: The Psychology of Persuasion" by Robert Cialdini**
   - Six principles: Reciprocity, Commitment/Consistency, Social Proof, Authority, Liking, Scarcity
   - Insight: I was using LOGIC to persuade when humans decide based on PSYCHOLOGY

2. **"Crucial Conversations" by Grenny, Patterson, et al.**
   - "Start with Heart" framework for high-stakes conversations
   - Making it safe for others to share views
   - Applied to recommendation approach: ask questions before presenting solutions

3. **"Made to Stick" by Chip and Dan Heath**
   - SUCCESs framework: Simple, Unexpected, Concrete, Credible, Emotional, Stories
   - Changed how I frame technical solutions: outcomes not outputs

4. **HBR Stakeholder Management Articles (2024-2025)**
   - Stakeholder adoption curve: Unaware → Resistant → Neutral → Supportive → Committed
   - Tailor communication to stakeholder position on curve

5. **"Thanks for the Feedback" by Douglas Stone and Sheila Heenen**
   - Three feedback triggers: Truth, Relationship, Identity
   - Reduced identity threats in recommendation framing

6. **YouTube: Communication Experts (Charlie Munger, Chris Voss, Simon Sinek)**
   - Pattern analysis: (1) Questions before answers, (2) Frame in their interests, (3) Use stories and examples

**Total Sources Analyzed:** 26+ (Books: 12, HBR articles: 5, YouTube/Expert content: 6, Research papers: 3)

**Key Frameworks Applied:**

**Stakeholder Adoption Curve:** Unaware → Resistant → Neutral → Supportive → Committed. The mistake is presenting solutions to "unaware" or "resistant" stakeholders. I now diagnose where stakeholders are and tailor communication accordingly.

**Cialdini's Principles Applied:**
- **Social Proof:** "Other agencies in your vertical are testing AI automation"
- **Commitment:** Start with small pilots before big commitments
- **Reciprocity:** Give value first before asking for partnership
- **Authority:** Reference case studies and research

**Questions-Before-Answers Pattern:** Every expert communicator studied follows this pattern. My old approach: present solution. New approach: ask questions first, understand context, co-create solution.

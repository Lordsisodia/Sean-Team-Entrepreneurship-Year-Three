## Competency

**What is the name and definition of this competency?**

Professional Communication: The ability to convey ideas effectively, influence stakeholders through strategic messaging, and create scalable communication systems that drive organisational success. This encompasses listening, persuasion, stakeholder management, and designing communication frameworks that work at scale.

**How is this competency relevant to my situation and SMART Objectives?**

Professional communication is fundamental to my Year 3 partnership scaling strategy. SMART Objective 4 (automated partnership outreach system targeting 75 conversations/week with 15% conversion) and Objective 6 (scaling from 100 to 500 partnerships) both depend on effective communication. The 100-partnership milestone achieved in April 2026 was driven by outreach conversations—manual outreach validated at 60 conversations/week. However, scaling to 500 partnerships requires systematic communication approaches, not just individual conversation skills.

This competency also connects to stakeholder influence. The LfO-04 undertaking revealed that only 40% of my Team Apollo recommendations were being adopted, despite being technically sound. This adoption gap wasn't a technical problem—it was a communication problem. I was providing solutions without ensuring buy-in, using logic when psychology drives decisions, and framing recommendations in my language rather than stakeholders' language. Addressing this communication gap is essential for leadership effectiveness and partnership success.

## Key Learning

One significant learning moment came from analysing why only 40% of my Team Apollo recommendations were being adopted (LfO-04, October-December 2025). The research revealed that I was making four critical mistakes: (1) Presenting solutions without building buy-in first, (2) Using logic when psychology drives decisions, (3) Framing solutions in my language, not theirs, and (4) Triggering identity threats by implying current approaches were "wrong." The profound insight was: "Adoption is not about the quality of your solution—it's about the quality of your communication process." This led to a complete communication framework redesign: Ask questions before giving answers. Listen before talking. Understand before solving. Frame solutions in outcomes ("helps you serve 10x more partners") not outputs ("multi-tenant architecture"). Start with "What have you tried? What concerns do you have?" rather than "Here's the solution."

Another key learning came from studying Cialdini's six principles of influence: Reciprocity, Commitment/Consistency, Social Proof, Authority, Liking, Scarcity. The insight was that I was trying to persuade through logic when humans decide based on psychology. I now apply these principles systematically to partnership conversations: Social Proof ("other agencies in your vertical are already testing AI automation"), Commitment (start with small pilots before asking for big commitments), Reciprocity (give value first—guides, insights, research—before asking for partnership), and Authority (reference case studies and research to build credibility). This psychology-based approach has improved both recommendation adoption and partnership conversion rates.

A third insight emerged from the "Made to Stick" SUCCESs framework (Simple, Unexpected, Concrete, Credible, Emotional, Stories). I learned that ideas stick when they're simple, concrete, and emotional—not complex, abstract, and logical. My technical recommendations were failing because they were too complex and abstract. People couldn't remember them or explain them to others. I now frame all communication in simple, concrete terms: Instead of "We need to refactor the authentication system for multi-tenant architecture," I say "This change lets you onboard 10x more partners without manual work." The first is complex and abstract; the second is simple, concrete, and emotional.

A fourth learning moment came from the Construction Deal Collapse experience (LbD-06, November-December 2025). The deal with Nick Mearson fell through after multiple conversations—it didn't end with a clear "no," it just faded away. Initially, I felt disappointed and questioned whether I was doing something wrong. But with perspective, I recognised that deal fallout is normal, not personal. Most deals end ambiguously, not with clear rejection. The insight was: "Deal fallout is normal. Rejection isn't about me—it's about fit, timing, and circumstances I can't control." This learning was essential for developing resilience in sales and business development. I continued outreach to other prospects despite the disappointment, maintaining the momentum that contributed to the 100-partnership achievement.

These experiences collectively shaped my understanding of professional communication. I moved from viewing communication as "transferring information" to viewing it as "ensuring adoption through psychology and systematic process." This shift is fundamental to both leadership effectiveness and partnership scaling.

## Progression through Practice

I progressed in professional communication through hands-on practice in three areas: stakeholder influence, partnership outreach, and systematic communication design.

**Stakeholder Influence (October-December 2025):** This involved applying the LfO-04 research to improve Team Apollo recommendation adoption. The old approach was: analyse problem → develop solution → present recommendation. The new approach is: ask questions → understand perspective → co-create solution → build buy-in → check for commitment. I applied the Crucial Conversations "Start with Heart" framework: know what you really want, then make it safe for others to share their views. I applied the stakeholder adoption curve: diagnose where stakeholders are (unaware → resistant → neutral → supportive → committed) and tailor communication accordingly. Early data shows improved adoption, though I'm still tracking results. The key metric shift is from "did I present the recommendation?" to "was the recommendation adopted?"

**Partnership Outreach (October 2025 - April 2026):** This involved conducting manual outreach at 60 conversations/week, contributing to the 100-partnership achievement (102 partners, £51K monthly revenue). I applied Cialdini's principles systematically: Social Proof ("other agencies are testing this"), Commitment (small pilots first), Reciprocity (giving value before asking), Authority (referencing case studies). I applied the "Made to Stick" framework: framing in simple, concrete, emotional terms ("helps you increase client retention") rather than technical language ("AI automation platform"). I applied the "questions first" principle: every partnership call starts with questions about their business, challenges, and goals. Solutions are framed in their language, not mine. The validation that manual outreach works at 60 conversations/week provides the foundation for the automated system in Objective 4.

**Systematic Communication Design (January 2026 onwards):** This involves building scalable communication systems rather than relying solely on individual conversation skills. I documented partnership communication templates: outreach email sequences, follow-up templates, objection handling scripts, and client communication frameworks. The goal is to systematise effective communication so it can scale beyond my personal capacity. This is the foundation for the automated outreach system targeting 75 conversations/week (Objective 4). The 35% progress rating reflects that basic templates and automation are built, but the full CRM integration and AI-assisted personalization are still in development.

**Dealing with Rejection (November-December 2025):** The Construction Deal Collapse experience (LbD-06) was a practice in emotional resilience and communication through ambiguity. The deal didn't end with a clear "no"—it just faded away. This is normal in business development. The learning was to separate my effort (which I can control) from the result (which I can't). I can do excellent work, communicate professionally, build rapport—and the deal may still fall through. That's not failure, it's business. This emotional detachment from outcomes is essential for sustaining the outreach volume required to hit 100 partnerships and eventually 500.

Putting professional communication into practice required confronting my own communication patterns and biases. I had to recognise that I was prioritising technical correctness over psychological effectiveness. I was prioritising being "right" over being influential. I was prioritising efficiency (quickly presenting the solution) over effectiveness (ensuring adoption). This self-awareness was uncomfortable but necessary for improvement.

Practicing this competency contributed to my overall development by teaching me that communication is a systematic skill that can be learned and improved, not just a natural talent. The progression from Year 2 (+25% satisfaction through individual skill development) to Year 3 (systematic communication frameworks and templates) represents the shift from artisan to architect—designing communication systems that scale.

## Progression through Enquiry

Enquiry-based learning was fundamental to developing my professional communication capability. The LfO-04 undertaking (October-December 2025) was a systematic study of influence, persuasion, and adoption psychology.

Core sources studied:

**"Influence: The Psychology of Persuasion" by Robert Cialdini:** This book provided the six principles that completely reframed my approach: Reciprocity, Commitment/Consistency, Social Proof, Authority, Liking, Scarcity. The insight was that I was using logic to persuade when humans decide based on psychology. I now apply these principles systematically to both Team Apollo recommendations and partnership conversations.

**"Crucial Conversations" by Grenny, Patterson, et al.:** This book taught me that when stakes are high and opinions differ, humans either go silent (avoidance) or violent (defensiveness). The solution is "Start with Heart"—know what you really want, then make it safe for others to share their views. My 40% adoption problem was because I was making conversations unsafe by coming in with "the answer." I now start with questions: "What are you seeing? What concerns do you have?"

**"Made to Stick" by Chip and Dan Heath:** This book provided the SUCCESs framework (Simple, Unexpected, Concrete, Credible, Emotional, Stories). The insight was that people remember what's simple, concrete, and emotional—not complex, abstract, and logical. My technical recommendations were failing because they were too complex. I now frame everything in simple, concrete outcomes.

**HBR Stakeholder Management Articles (2024-2025):** These articles taught me that stakeholder adoption follows a predictable curve: Unaware → Resistant → Neutral → Supportive → Committed. The mistake is presenting solutions to unaware or resistant stakeholders and expecting immediate adoption. I now diagnose where stakeholders are and tailor communication accordingly.

**"Thanks for the Feedback" by Douglas Stone and Sheila Heenen:** This book taught me about feedback triggers: Truth triggers ("that's wrong"), Relationship triggers ("who are you to say?"), and Identity triggers ("this means I'm bad at my job"). My recommendations were triggering identity threats by implying current approaches were "wrong." I now frame things as "here's a better approach" rather than "you're doing it wrong."

**YouTube Expert Analysis (Sinek, Voss, Munger):** Analysing 20+ expert communicators revealed three patterns: (1) They ask questions before giving answers, (2) They frame solutions in terms of the other person's interests, not their own, (3) They use stories and examples, not just abstract logic. This became my communication checklist before any important conversation.

Key insights from different sources:

**From adoption research:** The difference between "agreement" and "adoption." Agreement is nodding and saying "that sounds good." Adoption is actually changing behaviour and implementing the solution. I had been optimising for agreement when I should have been optimising for adoption.

**From sales psychology:** The importance of psychological safety in persuasion. People don't buy from people who make them feel stupid or wrong. They buy from people who make them feel smart and understood. This reinforced the "questions first" approach.

**From negotiation research (Chris Voss, "Never Split the Difference"):** The power of tactical empathy—acknowledging the other person's perspective before trying to influence them. "It sounds like you're concerned about X" is more persuasive than "You should do Y."

How this influenced my professional values and practices:

Communication research reinforced my commitment to psychology-first, logic-second communication. The old approach was: here's the logical solution, adopt it. The new approach is: here's what I understand about your situation, here's a solution framed in your interests, what do you think? This values shift from efficiency to effectiveness, from being right to being influential.

I also developed a stronger appreciation for systematic communication. Great communication isn't about being charismatic or articulate—it's about following frameworks that ensure adoption. Cialdini's principles, the Crucial Conversations approach, the stakeholder adoption curve—these are systematic approaches that anyone can learn and apply.

By integrating this knowledge, I've been able to improve both recommendation adoption and partnership communication. The early data from Team Apollo shows improved adoption. The 100-partnership achievement validates that the communication approach works in practice. The systematic templates and frameworks provide the foundation for the automated outreach system (Objective 4).

However, I recognise that communication development is ongoing. The 35% progress on Objective 4 reflects that there's still work to do to fully systematise communication for 500-partner scale. The transition from artisanal communication (individual skill) to industrial communication (systematic templates and automation) is still in progress.

## Competency

**What is the name and definition of this competency?**

Service Design & Delivery: The ability to create, implement, and continuously improve service offerings that deliver measurable value to customers, ensuring consistent quality at scale through systematic processes and customer-centric design.

**How is this competency relevant to my situation and SMART Objectives?**

Service design and delivery is the foundation of my Year 3 business strategy. My core business model—partnership-based AI automation—depends entirely on designing a service that agencies value and can successfully deliver to their clients. SMART Objective 6 (scaling from 100 to 500 partnerships) requires a systematic approach to service delivery, not ad-hoc client management. The 100-partner milestone achieved in April 2026, generating £51K monthly revenue, validates that the service model works. However, scaling to 500 partnerships demands that I transition from doing everything myself to designing systems that enable team-based delivery.

This competency is also critical to SMART Objective 5 (Client App MVP), which I completed with 100% satisfaction across 8 test clients. That experience taught me that service design isn't just about the product—it's about the entire customer journey from onboarding through ongoing support. The partnership scaling strategy builds directly on this learning: partnerships succeed only when agencies can successfully deliver the service to their end clients.

## Key Learning

One significant learning moment occurred during the Client App MVP delivery (October-December 2025). I had built the app with four industry templates (restaurant, bike rental, car rental, e-commerce), but I initially focused on technical features rather than the customer experience. After onboarding the first few test clients, I realised they were struggling not because the software was inadequate, but because I hadn't designed the service journey—onboarding, training, ongoing support, success metrics. This forced me to rapidly develop service blueprints: a 15-step onboarding checklist, weekly reporting templates, and clear success criteria. The result was 100% satisfaction (all 8 clients rated 9-10/10) and the recognition that service design encompasses the entire customer experience, not just the product.

Another key learning came from achieving the 100-partnership milestone (April 2026). Reaching 102 partnerships and £51K monthly revenue validated the service model, but what surprised me was that partnerships succeeded when I provided clear systems and struggled when I relied on ad-hoc communication. The partnerships where I followed structured onboarding, provided templates, and established clear reporting frameworks had dramatically higher success rates. This taught me that service quality at scale comes from systematic design, not individual effort. The partnerships that failed or struggled were typically ones where I had been reactive rather than proactive in designing the service experience.

A third insight emerged from reviewing the LbD undertakings that documented my client work (LbD-01 through LbD-06). Looking back at experiences like the Restaurant-to-Ecommerce Pivot and the Construction Deal Collapse, I realised that the most successful client engagements were ones where I had invested upfront in service design—understanding their needs, setting clear expectations, providing systems. The failures or challenges came when I skipped service design and jumped straight to execution. This retrospective learning reinforced that service design isn't a luxury or add-on—it's the foundation of successful delivery.

These experiences collectively shaped my understanding of service design and delivery. I moved from viewing service as "what I deliver to clients" to viewing it as "the systematic framework that ensures consistent value delivery." This shift in thinking is what enables me to now contemplate scaling from 100 to 500 partnerships—the focus is on designing systems that anyone can follow, not on individual heroics.

## Progression through Practice

I progressed in service design and delivery through hands-on practice in two major projects: the Client App MVP and the Partnership Scaling program.

The Client App MVP (October 2025 - January 2026) was my first systematic service design experience. The original objective was to build software, but it evolved into a service design challenge. I had to design: the onboarding process (how do clients get started successfully?), the training approach (how do they learn to use the app?), the ongoing support model (what happens when they have questions?), and the success metrics (how do we know they're achieving value?). I created the 15-step onboarding checklist, weekly reporting templates, and biweekly check-in cadence. The result was 100% client satisfaction and the creation of four industry-specific templates. More importantly, this experience taught me that clients succeed when the service experience is designed, not accidental.

The Partnership Scaling program (October 2025 - April 2026) was the second major practice. This involved designing the partnership service itself: outreach processes, qualification criteria, onboarding systems, ongoing support structures, and reporting frameworks. I validated that manual outreach works at 60 conversations per week (documented in Objective 4 progress). I documented partnership processes in the partnership app, including onboarding checklists, outreach email templates, and client communication scripts. The achievement of 102 partnerships and £51K monthly revenue validated that the service model works. However, this practice also revealed gaps: I need more systematic documentation and clearer delegation systems before I can scale to 500 partnerships.

Putting service design into practice required iterating based on feedback. For example, early partnerships suffered because I didn't provide enough structure. Partners didn't know what to expect, how to communicate, or what success looked like. I iterated by creating clearer templates, establishing weekly reporting cadences, and providing more upfront guidance. Later partnerships had significantly higher success rates because the service experience was more designed.

Practicing this competency contributed to my overall development by teaching me that scaling requires systematic design, not just hard work. The progression from Year 2 (managing projects through effort) to Year 3 (designing services that scale through systems) represents a fundamental shift in my approach to business. Service design and delivery is no longer something I do intuitively—it's something I design systematically.

## Progression through Enquiry

Enquiry-based learning was instrumental in developing my service design and delivery capability. I engaged with research, case studies, and frameworks that shaped my approach to designing scalable services.

Key frameworks studied included:

**Service Blueprinting:** I researched service design methodologies that map the entire customer journey across all touchpoints. This framework helped me design the Client App MVP experience—not just the software features, but the onboarding, training, support, and success metrics. I created visual service blueprints showing each step of the customer journey, which revealed gaps I hadn't noticed (e.g., clients needed templates, not just tools). This directly improved the 100% satisfaction rate.

**SaaS Unit Economics:** Through LfO-05 (Financial Thinking & Business Models), I studied how successful SaaS companies design services for scale. The key insight was that scalable services have positive unit economics (customer acquisition cost < lifetime value) and clear service tier structures. This influenced my partnership pricing model (£500/month validated through the £51K revenue achievement) and my thinking about the 500-partner target (£250K monthly revenue projection).

**Community-Led Growth:** Through LfO-02 (Partnership Development Strategies) and partnership scaling research, I learned that the most scalable companies use community to deliver service. Peer-to-peer support scales infinitely; 1:1 support doesn't. This influenced my plan to launch partnership program V3 with community features (Slack community, monthly webinars). The goal is to create a service model where partners support each other, reducing the burden on my team while improving satisfaction.

Key insights from different sources:

**From SaaS case studies:** I learned that companies successfully scale from 100 to 1,000 customers by (1) documenting all processes, (2) hiring customer success teams, (3) building automation, and (4) creating community. This directly informs my 500-partner scaling strategy (Objective 6).

**From service design research:** I discovered that service quality consistency comes from systematic design, not individual skill. The Ritz-Carlton's service design, for example, relies on detailed standards and employee empowerment, not just hiring great people. This reinforced the importance of documenting partnership processes.

**From client feedback:** The 100% satisfaction rate from Client App MVP clients provided qualitative insights about what works: clear expectations, proactive communication, easy-to-use templates, and responsive support. These insights are now built into the partnership service design.

How this influenced my professional values and practices:

Service design research reinforced my commitment to customer-centricity. The service isn't about what I want to deliver—it's about what creates value for the customer. This mindset shift is evident in how I now approach partnerships: I start by understanding the partner's business, their challenges, their goals. The service design flows from their needs, not my capabilities.

I also developed a stronger appreciation for systems thinking. Great service design isn't about individual heroics—it's about designing systems that make success easy and inevitable. This influences how I'm now approaching the 500-partner scaling goal: the focus is on systems, processes, and automation, not just working harder.

By integrating this knowledge, I've been able to design services that work at scale. The 102-partnership achievement and £51K monthly revenue validate that the service model is scalable. The challenge now is to refine the systems and documentation to enable team-based delivery as I scale from 100 to 500 partnerships.

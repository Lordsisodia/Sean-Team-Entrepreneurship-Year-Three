## Competency

**What is the name and definition of this competency?**

Service Design & Delivery: The ability to create, implement, and continuously improve service offerings that deliver measurable value to customers, ensuring consistent quality at scale through systematic processes and customer-centric design.

**How is this competency relevant to my situation and SMART Objectives?**

Service design competence is directly demonstrated by my client delivery work in October-November 2025. I delivered a complete restaurant ordering system to a paying client, including menu management, order tracking, and payment integration. The client was fully satisfied with the technical delivery. However, through client conversations, I discovered a critical insight: the restaurant segment lacks economic viability. The client told me directly: "We don't have many restaurant customers ourselves, we don't have much capital, and the website won't generate significant extra revenue for us."

This market intelligence triggered a strategic pivot to e-commerce clients, where I secured a £1,000 project (landing page + Shopify backend + mobile app). This progression demonstrates service design beyond technical delivery: understanding customer economics, business viability, and strategic positioning.

SMART Objective 5 targets completing a Client App MVP with 100% satisfaction across 5-10 test clients. The restaurant→ecommerce experience demonstrates service design thinking: validating customer segments, pivoting based on market evidence, and delivering solutions that align with client economics.

## Key Learning

One significant learning moment came from the restaurant client delivery (October 2025). I had built a complete ordering system with payment integration, menu management, and order tracking. The technical work was excellent - the client was satisfied with the product. However, through ongoing conversations, the client revealed their business reality: few restaurant customers, low capital, tight margins, and limited revenue potential from the automation investment.

The profound insight: **"Client feedback about their business situation is market intelligence, not just conversation."**

When the client explained their constraints, this wasn't just chat - it was data about an entire market segment. If one restaurant client has these economics, others likely do too. The restaurant segment isn't viable for premium automation services because restaurants lack capital and revenue potential.

Another key learning came from the strategic pivot itself. After 6+ weeks of restaurant-focused work, I had built templates, developed expertise, and created collateral. Abandoning this investment was emotionally difficult. However, the client data was clear. I learned: **"Sunk cost creates emotional attachment that must be overridden with data."**

The pivot to e-commerce wasn't just about technical transfer - the payment integration, order management, and mobile app patterns transferred directly. But the market understanding required re-learning. Restaurants have thin margins and capital constraints. E-commerce clients have proven revenue and technology budgets. Service design must account for customer economics, not just technical requirements.

A third insight emerged from the e-commerce client work itself. The £1,000 project went through 7-8 rounds of iteration based on client feedback. The learning: **"Service delivery is iterative co-creation, not one-way handoff."** The client provided feedback after each iteration. I implemented all requested changes without resistance. The client knows their business better than I do - my job is to enable their success, not defend my technical preferences.

## Progression through Enquiry

Enquiry-based learning focused on understanding what makes service systems scalable and customer segments viable.

**SaaS Unit Economics** (LfO-05: 18,557 bytes): Pricing strategy, unit economics, and business model validation. Understanding that CAC (Customer Acquisition Cost) must be recovered through LTV (Lifetime Value). Restaurants have low LTV due to thin margins - e-commerce clients have higher LTV.

**Value-Based Pricing** (LfO-05 research): Price based on value delivered, not cost incurred. £500/month represents roughly 10x ROI for partners (minimum £5K/month value delivered). The £1,000 e-commerce project was priced based on value to client, not hours worked.

**Service Blueprinting** (self-directed study): Methodologies mapping customer journeys across touchpoints. Influenced how I structure client engagements: understand customer economics first, design delivery system second, iterate based on feedback third.

**How this influenced professional values:**

Service design research reinforced commitment to systematic over ad-hoc approaches. The restaurant→ecommerce pivot validated that customer segment selection is as important as delivery excellence. Perfect technical execution doesn't compensate for poor customer economics.

The client qualification framework represents service design thinking applied upstream: design the customer selection criteria so that service delivery is sustainable. Good service design starts with saying no to wrong customers.

## Competency

**What is the name and definition of this competency?**

Strategic Leadership: The ability to envision and communicate a compelling future, build and develop high-performing teams, and create systems that enable scalable organisational success through others rather than individual effort.

**How is this competency relevant to my situation and SMART Objectives?**

Strategic leadership is critical because scaling from individual effort to team-based delivery requires learning to lead through systems, not just direct involvement. The evidence of my leadership development spans three years, with a documented failure in Year 3 producing the most profound learning.

My leadership journey represents a three-year progression: **Year 1 established the theoretical foundation** through systematic study of leadership principles; **Year 2 tested these principles through application** in scaling the AI Influencer project from 6 to over 100 influencers and managing intern onboarding; **Year 3 provided the crucible** - a real leadership failure that forced me to confront my limitations and grow.

The most significant leadership experience occurred from December 2025 to January 2026, when I attempted to delegate infrastructure setup and e-commerce client acquisition to Sam. I implemented a tracking system with £2.49/day fines for missed task tracking. After 60 days, both of us owed hundreds of pounds in fines, so we wrote off the system. This leadership failure taught me a critical lesson: "When systems fail, look at system design before blaming people." Sam successfully completed all assigned work - I never had to redo anything. The problem wasn't Sam's capability; it was my burdensome tracking system.

This experience demonstrates strategic leadership through vulnerability: acknowledging my design failure, learning that good systems make the right work easy (not just track wrong work), and recognising that Sam's capability validates the delegation strategy even if my first system failed.

SMART Objective 3 targets team-based delivery. The delegation attempt was the first step toward that goal, and the failure provided the systems-thinking foundation required for successful scaling.

## Progression Summary

### Three-Year Leadership Trajectory

**Year 1 (2023-24): Theoretical Foundation**

At the start of Year 1, I engaged in systematic study of leadership principles through multiple LfO undertakings. These established my foundational understanding of what effective leadership requires:

- **"7 Habits of Highly Effective People"** (Covey) taught me proactivity - taking initiative and responsibility rather than waiting for circumstances to dictate action. The "Begin with the End in Mind" habit provided a framework for visionary leadership, while "First Things First" taught prioritization. The "Think Win-Win" principle informed my approach to collaborative leadership.

- **"Exponential Organizations 2.0"** (Ismael, Diamandis, Malone) introduced me to 10X thinking and the SCALE framework. This expanded my understanding of what's possible when leaders think exponentially rather than linearly, and how to design organisations that leverage network effects and massive transformative purpose.

- **"Leading Self Towards Awareness"** workshop developed my self-awareness and emotional intelligence. I learned that effective leadership begins with self-leadership - understanding my own strengths, weaknesses, values, and emotional patterns before attempting to lead others.

By the end of Year 1, I had built a strong theoretical understanding of leadership principles but had limited practical application. My assessment placed me at Intermediate level - I understood the concepts but hadn't yet been tested in real leadership situations.

**Year 2 (2024-25): Application and Testing**

Year 2 provided my first significant leadership challenges. I took on the ambitious goal of scaling the AI Influencer project from 6 to over 100 influencers, which required me to lead a growing team through complex coordination and communication challenges.

Key leadership experiences in Year 2 included:

- **Team Expansion:** Managing the onboarding of multiple interns while simultaneously scaling operations. This required adapting my leadership style to accommodate a growing and diverse team, needing more structured communication, clear goal-setting, and effective delegation.

- **Cross-Functional Leadership:** Leading a cross-functional team on a collaborative project. The team comprised individuals from diverse backgrounds with different working styles. Initially, this diversity led to conflicts and misunderstandings. I learned to adapt my leadership style to be more inclusive, encouraging open communication and leveraging each person's strengths.

- **High-Stakes Leadership:** Leading through tight deadlines on high-stakes projects. When the team was overwhelmed and morale was low, I had to step up, provide reassurance, outline a clear achievable plan, and refocus efforts. This taught me the importance of leadership in maintaining focus, energy, and commitment during challenging times.

- **Mentorship Development:** I took on a more active role in mentoring new team members, sharing my experiences and guiding them through their professional development. This taught me that leadership is not just about directing others but about creating environments where people can thrive.

By the end of Year 2, I had progressed to Advanced level. I had successfully led complex projects and managed teams through challenging situations. However, I recognised that delegation remained my critical challenge - I was still doing too much myself and hadn't yet learned to scale leadership through systems rather than individual effort.

**Year 3 (2025-26): Mastery Through Failure**

Year 3 began with my assessment at Advanced level, recognising delegation as the critical gap between my current capability and Expert level. The year provided the crucible that would test and transform my leadership approach.

The delegation experience (December 2025 - January 2026) revealed that I understood accountability in theory but created a burdensome system in practice. The £2.49/day fine system failed because it was easier to skip tracking than to do it daily. Sam and I both found the system burdensome, and the accumulated fines became a joke rather than a motivator.

However, throughout the 60 days, Sam successfully completed all the infrastructure and client acquisition work he was assigned. I never had to redo anything. This was a profound leadership realisation: Sam is capable, but the system I designed was terrible. The failure was entirely mine as a leader - I created a tracking system that was burdensome, then blamed people for not using it.

This experience revealed that the challenge isn't trusting others' capability - it's designing systems that enable success rather than policing failure. My initial assessment was accurate in identifying delegation as core challenge, but I learned that the gap was in systems design, not trust.

By the end of Year 3, I progressed toward Expert level. The vulnerability of acknowledging my leadership failure to Sam builds trust and shows I take responsibility for system design failures. The shift from "Sam failed at tracking" to "I failed at system design" represents fundamental growth in leadership thinking.

## Key Learning

### Three-Year Learning Evolution

**Year 1 Learning: Theoretical Frameworks**

The foundation of my leadership understanding was built through systematic engagement with leadership theory in Year 1:

- **Proactivity (7 Habits):** I learned that effective leaders are proactive, not reactive - they take initiative and responsibility rather than waiting for circumstances to dictate action. This principle has informed my approach to every leadership challenge since.

- **Visionary Thinking (Exponential Organizations):** The concept of "Begin with the End in Mind" from 7 Habits combined with 10X thinking from Exponential Organizations to give me tools for strategic vision. I learned that leaders must envision compelling futures and think exponentially about what's possible.

- **Self-Awareness (Leading Self workshop):** Developing emotional intelligence and self-awareness taught me that leadership begins with understanding myself - my values, triggers, patterns, and limitations. This self-knowledge has been crucial in managing my reactions under pressure.

- **Empowerment (7 Habits - Synergize):** The principle of synergizing - that the whole is greater than the sum of its parts - informed my approach to building collaborative teams and leveraging diverse strengths.

This theoretical foundation was essential but incomplete. I understood what good leadership looked like in principle but hadn't yet been tested in real high-stakes situations.

**Year 2 Learning: Application Through Challenge**

Year 2 threw me into leadership situations where theory met practice:

- **Adaptive Leadership:** Leading the AI Influencer expansion required adapting my leadership style as the team grew. What worked for 6 people didn't work for 100. I learned to be more directive when necessary and more delegating as team members demonstrated capability.

- **Crisis Leadership:** The high-stakes project where morale was low taught me that leadership in difficult moments is about providing reassurance, clarity, and hope. I learned to break down overwhelming challenges into clear achievable steps.

- **Diversity Management:** The cross-functional team experience taught me that diverse teams require inclusive leadership. I learned to adapt my communication style, create space for different voices, and leverage diversity as a strength rather than seeing it as a source of conflict.

- **Leadership as Facilitation:** Mentoring new team members shifted my role from directing to facilitating. I learned that effective leadership brings out the best in others rather than controlling every action.

Year 2 reinforced the principles learned in Year 1 while revealing gaps. I was successful in situations where I could be directly involved, but I recognised that scaling to 100+ influencers required delegation and systems - my critical challenge.

**Year 3 Learning: Transformation Through Failure**

The Year 3 delegation experience provided the most profound leadership learning of my three-year journey:

**The Delegation Experience (December 2025 - January 2026):**

One significant learning moment is documented in LbD-04-Delegation-to-Sam.md (1,845 words). I attempted to delegate infrastructure setup and client acquisition to Sam, implementing a daily task tracking system with £2.49/day fines for missed tracking. After 60 days, both Sam and I owed hundreds of pounds in fines, so we wrote off the system.

The profound insight: **"When systems fail, look at system design before blaming people."**

Sam successfully completed all the infrastructure and client acquisition work - I never had to redo anything. The problem wasn't Sam's capability or motivation - it was my system design. I created a tracking system that was burdensome, and then blamed people for not using it.

**Key Insight 1:** **"Extrinsic motivation (financial penalties) doesn't work when the process itself is difficult. Good systems make the right behavior easy, not just punished when wrong."**

The tracking system failed because it was easier to skip tracking than to do it daily. The £2.49 penalty was meant to incentivise tracking, but the real problem was that the tracking system itself was too cumbersome. I should have designed a system that was easy to use, not one with penalties for non-use. This connects to Year 1 learning from 7 Habits about "First Things First" - I prioritized tracking over usability, which violated the principle of making the right thing the easy thing.

**Key Insight 2:** **"Never having to redo Sam's work proves he's capable. The bottleneck is my system design, not his performance."**

This validated my decision to delegate to Sam. He successfully completed infrastructure setup and client acquisition work without rework. The failure was entirely in the tracking system, not the operational work. This means Sam can handle responsibility if I provide better processes.

**Key Insight 3:** **"Vulnerability is strength. Leaders who can't admit mistakes don't learn from them."**

I told Sam explicitly that the tracking system was my mistake, not his. This honesty builds trust and shows I take responsibility for system design failures. The vulnerability of acknowledging this failure is itself evidence of leadership growth. This connects to Year 1 "Leading Self" workshop learning about self-awareness and emotional intelligence - being vulnerable requires the self-awareness to recognise my mistakes and the emotional intelligence to handle the discomfort of admission.

**Systems Thinking Integration:**

The delegation failure taught me about systems thinking, connecting to Year 2 research in LfO-03 on "High Output Management" and "The Phoenix Project." I learned that my leadership failure was a system design problem, not a Sam performance problem. The bottleneck was my system design, not his performance.

This also connects to Year 1 "7 Habits" principle of "Sharpen the Saw" - I need to continuously renew and improve my leadership capabilities. The delegation experience was a painful but necessary "sharpening" experience.

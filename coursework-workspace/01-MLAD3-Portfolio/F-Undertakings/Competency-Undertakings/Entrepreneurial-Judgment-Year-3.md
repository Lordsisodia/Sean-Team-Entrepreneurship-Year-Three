## Competency

**What is the name and definition of this competency?**

Entrepreneurial Judgment: The ability to make strategic decisions under uncertainty, validate assumptions before significant investment, allocate resources to highest-impact opportunities based on evidence rather than speculation, and balance ambition with realistic assessment. This encompasses evidence-based decision-making, opportunity cost analysis, strategic patience, and honest self-assessment.

**How is this competency relevant to my situation and SMART Objectives?**

Entrepreneurial judgment is the meta-competency that informs all strategic decisions. It's the difference between smart scaling and premature scaling, between evidence-based ambition and speculative aspiration. The progression from SMART Objective 6 original target (100 partnerships, achieved April 2026) to the updated stretch goal (500 partnerships, target November 2026) required entrepreneurial judgment: the 100-partner milestone validated the business model, providing the evidence base to commit to the 500-partner target.

This competency also connects to target adjustments across objectives. The cannabis-free target adjustment (100% → 80%, Objective 1) and the focused hours adjustment (70 → 50 per week, Objective 1) both demonstrate entrepreneurial judgment: recognising that perfect targets aren't achievable during rapid scaling, and adjusting to realistic targets that maintain ambition while acknowledging business reality. Honest self-assessment—even when it reveals gaps—is a key aspect of entrepreneurial judgment.

## Key Learning

One significant learning moment came from the 100-partner milestone achievement (April 2026). Reaching 102 partnerships and £51K monthly revenue wasn't just a goal achievement—it was a validation point. I had the data to confirm that the business model works: £500/month pricing validated by market adoption, unit economics positive (CAC:LTV healthy), operational capacity proven (manual outreach at 60 conversations/week), team development started (Sam at 12+ partnerships). The insight was: "Strategic decisions require evidence validation, not speculative projections. Validating the 100-partner model before committing to 500 partners prevents premature scaling." This evidence-based approach is directly contrasted with the Year 2 mistake (the 6-week internal app) where I committed resources without validation. The learning was that patience to validate first prevents expensive mistakes later.

Another key learning came from the target adjustments (November 2025 - January 2026). The time-tracking app data revealed ~50% actual compliance vs the 100% cannabis-free target. I could have continued claiming 100% while missing the target, or I could adjust to an honest 80% target that reflects reality while maintaining ambition. I chose the latter. Similarly, the focused hours target shifted from 70 to 50 per week after recognising burnout signals. The insight was: "Entrepreneurial judgment requires honest self-assessment. Lying to yourself destroys judgment capability. Data > ego." These adjustments weren't about lowering standards—they were about aligning targets with reality so they can actually drive improvement rather than pretense.

A third insight emerged from the opportunity cost analysis required by strategic focus. In October-December 2025, I had multiple potential opportunities: new product lines, market expansions, partnership focus. I had limited resources (my time, attention). I needed to choose the highest-impact opportunity. I declined new product opportunities and focused exclusively on partnership scaling. The result: 102 partnerships achieved, £51K monthly revenue. The insight was: "Entrepreneurial judgment = saying no to good opportunities to focus on great opportunities. Success comes from strategic focus, not doing everything." The opportunity cost framework—what am I giving up by choosing this?—became a key decision tool.

A fourth learning moment came from reviewing the Construction Deal Collapse experience (LbD-06, November-December 2025). The deal with Nick Mearson fell through after multiple conversations. Initially, I felt disappointed and questioned whether I was doing something wrong. But with perspective, I recognised that deal fallout is normal, not personal. The insight was: "Entrepreneurial judgment includes emotional resilience. Deals fall through for reasons beyond your control. Rejection isn't about your competence—it's about fit, timing, and circumstances." This emotional detachment from outcomes—the ability to separate effort (which I can control) from results (which I can't)—is essential for sustaining the outreach volume required for 100-partnership success and eventually 500.

These experiences collectively shaped my understanding of entrepreneurial judgment. I moved from viewing decisions as binary (right/wrong) to viewing them as probabilistic (what's the likelihood this will work, based on what evidence?). The progression from Year 2 (speculative execution, 6-week mistake) to Year 3 (evidence-based validation, 100-partner milestone before 500-partner commitment) represents significant development in judgment quality.

## Progression through Practice

I progressed in entrepreneurial judgment through hands-on practice in four areas: evidence-based scaling, target adjustment, opportunity cost prioritisation, and learning from mistakes.

**Evidence-Based Scaling (September 2025 - April 2026):** This involved validating the 100-partner model before committing to the 500-partner target. The progression was: set 100-partner target → execute with focus → achieve milestone (April 2026) → validate model → commit to 500-partner stretch goal. The validation criteria included: pricing model validated (£500/month × 102 partners = £51K MRR), unit economics positive (CAC:LTV healthy), operational capacity proven (manual outreach at 60 conversations/week), team development feasible (Sam at 12+ partnerships, first hire January 2026). Only after this validation did I commit to the 500-partner target. This evidence-based approach directly contrasts with the Year 2 mistake (premature execution without validation). The strategic patience—waiting to validate before scaling—required resisting the constant temptation to grow faster.

**Target Adjustment with Integrity (November 2025 - January 2026):** This involved adjusting SMART objective targets based on honest evidence rather than ego. The time-tracking app (Objective 2, 85% progress) provided data: ~50% actual compliance on cannabis-free habit vs 100% target. I could have ignored this data and continued claiming 100% compliance. Instead, I adjusted the target to 80%—still ambitious but acknowledging business reality. Similarly, the focused hours target shifted from 70 to 50 per week after recognising sustainable capacity vs maximum effort. These adjustments demonstrate entrepreneurial judgment through realistic self-assessment. The key principle: "Honest data > false perfection. Targets should stretch capability, not deny reality."

**Opportunity Cost Prioritisation (October 2025 - December 2025):** This involved choosing the highest-impact focus when multiple opportunities were available. I evaluated: new product lines (AI tools), market expansion (international), partnership focus (100 partners). I used an analysis framework: revenue potential, execution complexity, time to market, strategic fit. Partnership focus scored highest on all dimensions. I declined the other opportunities and focused exclusively on partnership scaling. The result: 102 partnerships achieved, £51K monthly revenue. The insight was that strategic focus (saying no to good opportunities) drives success more than doing everything. The opportunity cost analysis—what am I giving up by choosing this?—became a standard part of decision-making.

**Learning from Mistakes Without Overcorrection:** The Year 2 internal app mistake (6-week wasted effort) could have made me risk-averse. Instead, I learned to distinguish between speculative risks (unvalidated assumptions) and calculated risks (validated assumptions). The 100-partner target was a calculated risk based on partial validation (manual outreach testing). The 500-partner target is also a calculated risk based on full validation (100-partner milestone achieved). The learning was: "Don't avoid risk—manage risk through validation. Learning from mistakes doesn't mean becoming risk-averse; it means making calculated bets based on evidence."

Putting entrepreneurial judgment into practice required confronting uncomfortable truths. The time-tracking data didn't lie. I could choose to ignore it (maintain the illusion of perfect compliance) or use it (confront reality and improve). I chose to use it. This commitment to truth-telling, even when it reveals gaps, is essential for good judgment. You can't make good decisions if you're lying to yourself about current reality.

Practicing this competency contributed to my overall development by teaching me that judgment quality comes from evidence-based processes, not individual brilliance. The progression from Year 2 (gut-feel decisions, expensive mistakes) to Year 3 (systematic validation, evidence-based commitment) represents the shift from intuitive to analytical decision-making.

## Progression through Enquiry

Enquiry-based learning was essential for developing my entrepreneurial judgment capability. The LfO research undertakings (LfO-01 through LfO-06, October 2025 - January 2026) provided frameworks for strategic decision-making across multiple domains.

Key frameworks studied:

**Lean Startup Methodology (Eric Ries):** I learned the Build-Measure-Learn cycle: Minimum Viable Product → measure outcomes → learn → iterate. Validated learning > vanity metrics. Fail fast, fail cheap, learn faster. I applied this to partnership scaling: validate 100-partner model (MVP scale) before 500-partner commitment. This framework directly prevented repeating the Year 2 premature scaling mistake. The strategic patience—waiting for validation—is the application of Lean Startup thinking.

**First Principles Thinking:** I learned to break problems down to fundamental truths and build up from there, rather than reasoning by analogy. I applied this to the capacity planning question: what is sustainable work capacity? Not "what do other entrepreneurs do?" (analogy) but "what does the data show about my actual capacity?" (first principles). The answer: 50 focused hours/week sustainable, not 70 hours maximum. This first-principles analysis led to realistic target setting.

**Opportunity Cost Analysis:** I studied economic principles applied to entrepreneurship. Every decision has opportunity cost—resources are finite. The best opportunity isn't just good in absolute terms; it's better than alternatives. I applied this framework to the strategic focus decision: partnership focus vs new products vs market expansion. Partnership focus scored highest on revenue potential, execution feasibility, and strategic fit. The result: 102 partnerships achieved. The opportunity cost framework is now a standard part of decision-making.

**Validated Learning:** I learned that learning is only "validated" when tested through empirical experimentation. Opinions and hypotheses are not evidence. The LfO research undertakings tested assumptions with real data. The 100-partner milestone validated the business model with actual partners and actual revenue. This validation is what enables the 500-partner commitment. The distinction between opinion (I think this will work) and evidence (this has worked) is fundamental to entrepreneurial judgment.

**Strategic Patience:** I studied case studies of scaling successes and failures. The common pattern: successful companies validate before scale; failed companies scale before validation. The strategic patience to validate 100-partner model before 500-partner commitment requires resisting growth pressure. But premature scaling is more dangerous than slow growth. This principle—validate then scale—guided the Year 3 strategy.

How this influenced my professional values and practices:

Strategic decision-making research reinforced my commitment to evidence-based processes. The old approach might have been: set ambitious targets, figure out execution. The new approach is: validate at small scale, then commit to scale. The 100-partner milestone was the validation point; the 500-partner target is the scaling commitment. This evidence-based approach is slower but more sustainable.

I also developed a stronger appreciation for systematic decision-making frameworks. Entrepreneurial judgment isn't about gut instinct or intuition—it's about following processes that increase the probability of good outcomes. The validation process, the opportunity cost analysis, the honest self-assessment—these are systematic approaches that anyone can apply.

By integrating this knowledge, I've been able to make better strategic decisions. The evidence-based scaling approach (validate 100 before 500) prevented repeating the Year 2 mistake. The honest target adjustments (80% vs 100%, 50 vs 70 hours) demonstrate realistic self-assessment. The strategic focus (partnerships vs other opportunities) enabled the 102-partner achievement.

However, I recognise that entrepreneurial judgment development is ongoing. The true test will be whether the judgment quality holds as challenges increase in complexity. 500-partner scaling will bring new decisions that require good judgment: hiring choices, pricing evolution, competitive responses, market changes. The frameworks and habits developed in Year 3 will be tested.

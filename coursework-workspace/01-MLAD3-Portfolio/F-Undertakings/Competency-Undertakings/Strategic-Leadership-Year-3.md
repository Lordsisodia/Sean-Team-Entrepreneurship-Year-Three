## Competency

**What is the name and definition of this competency?**

Strategic Leadership: The ability to envision and communicate a compelling future, build and develop high-performing teams, and create systems that enable scalable organisational success through others rather than individual effort.

Strategic leadership is critical to my Year 3 goals because scaling from 100 to 500 partnerships (Objective 6) requires building a team, not just working harder. SMART Objective 3 specifically targets hiring and training two partnership managers, with Sam managing 50+ partnerships by August 2026. This competency also connects to my broader exit strategy. Building a partnership-based AI automation business that can operate without my daily involvement requires designing organisational systems, not just doing work myself. The progress I've made in Year 3—hiring my first partnership manager in January 2026, building basic process documentation, and developing Sam from zero partnerships to managing 12+—represents the foundation of this transition. ## Key Learning

One significant learning moment occurred during the Delegation to Sam undertaking (December 2025 - January 2026). I had implemented a daily task tracking system with £2.49/day fines for missed tracking, believing that financial accountability would drive compliance. After 60 days, both Sam and I owed hundreds of pounds in fines, so we wrote off the system. The profound insight was: "When systems fail, look at system design before blaming people." Sam successfully completed all the infrastructure and client acquisition work he was assigned—I never had to redo anything. The problem wasn't Sam's capability or motivation; it was my system design. I had created a tracking system that was burdensome, then blamed people for not using it. This taught me that leadership is about designing systems that make the right work easy, not just imposing consequences for the wrong work.

Another key learning came from researching strategic communication and influence (LfO-04, October-December 2025). My analysis revealed that only 40% of my Team Apollo recommendations were being adopted, despite being technically sound. The research—spanning Cialdini's "Influence," "Crucial Conversations," and HBR stakeholder management articles—taught me that adoption is not about the quality of your solution but about the quality of your communication process. I was using logic when humans decide based on psychology. I was presenting solutions without building buy-in first. I was framing solutions in my language, not theirs. This insight fundamentally changed my leadership approach: I now start with questions ("What are you seeing? What concerns do you have?") rather than answers ("Here's the solution").

A third insight emerged from the hiring process for my first partnership manager (January 2026). The £50K monthly revenue from 102 partnerships gave me the financial capacity to hire, but I realised that hiring before having documented systems would be irresponsible. The LbD-04 experience had taught me that Sam's capability wasn't the bottleneck—my systems were. This led to the recognition that strategic leadership requires designing the organisational systems before scaling headcount. The hire was made in January 2026, but only after I had documented basic partnership processes: onboarding checklists, outreach templates, and communication scripts.

These experiences collectively shaped my understanding of strategic leadership. I moved from viewing leadership as "directing people to do work" to viewing it as "designing systems that enable others to succeed." This shift is fundamental to scaling from 100 to 500 partnerships. The progression from Year 2 (struggling with delegation) to Year 3 (systematically building team capability) represents significant development, but I recognise that I'm still early in the journey. The true test will be whether the systems I'm building can enable a team of three partnership managers to operate without my daily involvement.

## Progression through Practice

I progressed in strategic leadership through hands-on practice in three areas: team development, organisational design, and stakeholder influence.

**Team Development (October 2025 - January 2026):** This involved developing Sam from zero partnership experience to managing 12+ partnerships independently. The progression happened in phases: Phase 1 (October-November) involved direct supervision—Sam shadowing me, joint calls, immediate feedback. Phase 2 (November-December) involved guided independence—Sam managing 3-6 partnerships with weekly review sessions. Phase 3 (December-January) involved independent execution—Sam managing 12+ partnerships with weekly check-ins only. The key learning was that development follows a clear trajectory: direct supervision → guided practice → independent execution. However, the failed tracking system experiment (LbD-04) taught me that delegation requires systems that are easy to use, not just systems that are comprehensive. Sam successfully completed all assigned work (I never had to redo anything), proving capability isn't the bottleneck—system design is.

**Organisational Design (January 2026 onwards):** Hiring the first partnership manager in January 2026 marked the transition from individual contributor to organisational builder. I documented partnership processes in the partnership app: onboarding checklists, outreach email templates, client communication scripts. The goal is to create organisational memory that doesn't depend on me. This practice taught me that organisations scale through systems, not individual effort. The 25% progress rating on Objective 3 reflects both progress (first hire made, Sam at 12+ partnerships) and the long journey ahead (need to hire 2nd partnership manager, Sam needs to reach 50+ partnerships, all processes need documentation).

**Stakeholder Influence (October 2025 - December 2025):** The LfO-04 undertaking on Strategic Communication & Influence was a practice in influence without authority. The 40% adoption rate of my Team Apollo recommendations revealed that I was providing technical direction without ensuring adoption. The research and application changed my approach: (1) Ask questions before giving answers, (2) Frame solutions in terms of the other person's interests, not mine, (3) Use stories and concrete examples, not abstract logic. I applied Cialdini's principles of influence (social proof, commitment, reciprocity) to partnership conversations. Early data shows improved adoption, though I'm still tracking results.

Putting strategic leadership into practice required confronting my own control tendencies. The LbD-04 experience was painful but necessary—I had to acknowledge that the tracking system failure was my leadership failure, not Sam's performance failure. I explicitly told Sam this, which built trust. Vulnerability and admitting mistakes are not weaknesses in leadership—they're essential for building psychological safety.

Practicing this competency contributed to my overall development by forcing me to confront the difference between individual contribution and organisational impact. In Year 2, I measured my contribution by what I personally accomplished. In Year 3, I'm learning to measure my contribution by what the team accomplishes. This represents a fundamental identity shift that's essential for scaling.

## Progression through Enquiry

Enquiry-based learning was critical for developing my strategic leadership capability. I engaged with frameworks, research, and case studies that shaped my approach to building teams and influencing stakeholders.

Key frameworks studied:

**Hersey-Blanchard Situational Leadership Model:** This framework from Year 2 continued to inform my approach in Year 3. The model argues that leadership style should match follower readiness: Directing (for beginners) → Coaching (for learners) → Supporting (for developing performers) → Delegating (for experts). I applied this systematically to Sam's development: Phase 1 was Directing (Sam shadowing me), Phase 2 was Coaching (Sam managing partnerships with guidance), Phase 3 was Supporting (Sam managing independently with check-ins). The goal is Phase 4 (Delegating) where Sam can mentor new hires. This framework provided the roadmap for Sam's progression from 0 to 12+ partnerships.

**Cialdini's Six Principles of Influence:** From "Influence: The Psychology of Persuasion," I learned that influence is based on psychology, not logic. The six principles—Reciprocity, Commitment/Consistency, Social Proof, Authority, Liking, Scarcity—explain why recommendations get adopted or ignored. My 40% adoption problem was because I was using logic when humans decide based on psychology. I now apply these principles to partnership conversations: social proof ("other agencies in your vertical are testing this"), commitment (start with small pilots), reciprocity (give value first), and authority (reference case studies and research).

**Crucial Conversations Framework:** This book taught me that when stakes are high and opinions differ, humans either go silent or violent. The solution is "Start with Heart"—know what you really want, then make it safe for others to share their views. My 40% adoption problem was likely because I was making conversations unsafe by coming in with "the answer." I changed my approach to start with questions: "What are you seeing? What concerns do you have?" This makes it safe for others to participate in the solution.

**Stakeholder Adoption Curve:** From HBR stakeholder management articles, I learned that adoption follows a predictable curve: Unaware → Resistant → Neutral → Supportive → Committed. The mistake is presenting solutions to "unaware" or "resistant" stakeholders and expecting immediate adoption. I now diagnose where stakeholders are on the curve and tailor communication accordingly. For "unaware" stakeholders, I focus on education. For "resistant" stakeholders, I focus on listening. For "neutral" stakeholders, I use social proof. This framework has improved my recommendation adoption rate.

Key insights from different sources:

**From "High Output Management" by Andy Grove:** I learned that accountability systems should enable performance, not create bureaucracy. The tracking system failure (LbD-04) violated this principle—I created a burdensome system that felt like policing, not enablement. Good management systems make the right work easy, not just track the wrong work.

**From SaaS leadership case studies:** I learned that companies successfully scale from founder-led to team-led by (1) documenting all processes, (2) hiring for culture fit and capability, (3) investing in training and development, and (4) creating clear career progression. This directly informs my hiring and team development strategy for Objective 3.

**From the LfO research on leadership automation (LfO-03):** I learned that leadership at scale requires automated systems, not just individual capability. Performance dashboards, automated reporting, and systematic communication cadences enable leaders to manage larger teams without becoming bottlenecks.

How this influenced my professional values and practices:

Strategic leadership research reinforced my commitment to empowerment over control. The tracking system failure taught me that control-based leadership (financial penalties, burdensome tracking) doesn't work. Empowerment-based leadership (clear processes, easy systems, natural accountability) does. This values shift is evident in how I'm now approaching delegation: I'm focusing on process documentation that makes success obvious, not tracking systems that feel like policing.

I also developed a stronger appreciation for systems thinking in leadership. Great leadership isn't about being charismatic or inspirational—it's about designing organisational systems that produce results. The progression from founder-led (100 partnerships) to team-led (500 partnerships) requires systematic organisational design, not just hiring good people.

By integrating this knowledge, I've been able to make progress in team development despite the challenges. Sam's progression from 0 to 12+ partnerships in 4 months, the first hire made in January 2026, and the basic process documentation completed all represent application of these leadership frameworks. However, I recognise that I'm still early in the journey. The true test will be whether these systems enable a team of three to manage 500 partnerships without my daily involvement.

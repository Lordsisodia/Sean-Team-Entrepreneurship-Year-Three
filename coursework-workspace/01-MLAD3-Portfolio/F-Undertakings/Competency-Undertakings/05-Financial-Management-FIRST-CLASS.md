## Key Learning

One significant learning came from the restaurant client pricing experience (October 2025). I delivered a complete ordering system but underpriced the work relative to value. The restaurant economics (thin margins, limited capital) constrained pricing ability.

The profound insight: **"Customer segment selection determines pricing power. You can't charge premium prices to customers who can't afford them."**

The restaurant client revealed their economics directly: "We don't have many customers, we don't have much capital, and the website won't generate significant revenue." This market intelligence led to the e-commerce pivot, where clients have proven revenue and technology budgets.

Another key learning came from LfO-05 research on value-based pricing. The insight: **"Price based on value delivered, not cost incurred. £500/month represents roughly 10x ROI for partners (minimum £5K/month value delivered)."**

The £1,000 e-commerce project was priced based on this principle. The client has revenue and technology budgets - they can afford to pay for value. The restaurant client couldn't afford premium pricing because their economics don't support it.

A third insight emerged from unit economics analysis. LfO-05 documents CAC (Customer Acquisition Cost), LTV (Lifetime Value), and CAC:LTV ratios. The learning: **"Business model validation requires actual data, not projections. Unit economics must be positive before scaling commitment."**

This principle informs the scaling strategy: validate partnership economics at 100 partners before committing to 500. Bootstrap financing requires positive economics - you can't scale without unit profitability.

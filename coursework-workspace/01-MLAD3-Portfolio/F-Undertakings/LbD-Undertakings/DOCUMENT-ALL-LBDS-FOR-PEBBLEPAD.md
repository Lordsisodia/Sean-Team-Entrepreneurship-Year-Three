## 1. PURPOSE (150 words max)

This Learning by Doing undertaking addresses my Entrepreneurial Judgment and Financial Management competencies through real business model analysis and market validation. In October 2025, I built a restaurant automation app with Next.js and Supabase, believing it would demonstrate technical excellence and create partnership opportunities. 

However, after analyzing customer conversations and market research, I discovered a critical flaw: restaurant operators operate with thin margins (£100-200 daily revenue) and minimal capital, making them fundamentally unviable for subscription-based automation services. Simultaneously, I had been developing an e-commerce template with multi-tenant architecture for bike rental agencies, which showed vastly superior economics (30-50% margins, customers with technology budgets and proven willingness to pay for value-added services).

The learning was confronting a painful assumption: that technical excellence and feature development equals business success. I had to choose between continuing on a broken path (restaurant automation) or pivoting to a viable business model (e-commerce). The restaurant-to-e-commerce pivot required abandoning £5,000 of sunk costs and confronting the reality that my initial business model was flawed.

This directly addresses my Entrepreneurial Judgment competency: I learned to validate business models through systematic customer research and financial analysis rather than relying on intuition or technical aesthetics. The pivot decision-making process—evaluating unit economics, understanding customer acquisition costs, and projecting revenue potential—demonstrates strategic resource allocation under uncertainty.

It also addresses Financial Management: By studying SaaS pricing frameworks (Revenue Per Available Seat Hour, customer lifetime value, unit economics calculations with CAC, LTV, churn rate), I developed financial modeling capabilities. Understanding the dramatic difference between restaurant economics (low margins, high operational complexity) and e-commerce economics (high margins, lower complexity) was essential for making informed business model choices and pricing strategy decisions.

## 4. CRITICAL EVALUATION OF LEARNING (900 words max)

### WHAT? (The Learning Experience)

The restaurant-to-e-commerce pivot represented a profound learning experience about business model validation and strategic decision-making under uncertainty. I faced tension between technical excellence (building restaurant automation app) and business viability (restaurants are fundamentally broken for partnership automation). The decision required confronting a painful reality: my initial business model was flawed, and continuing would waste time and resources.

### SO WHAT? (How This Changed My Thinking)

**Initial assumption challenged:** I believed that "technical excellence = business success." Building a perfectly engineered restaurant app with Next.js, Supabase, and TypeScript would automatically create partnership opportunities. This was activity-focused productivity masquerading as strategic effectiveness.

**What I discovered:** Restaurant economics are fundamentally broken for partnership services. Operators have low revenue (£100-200/day) and minimal capital (£1K-£10K), making SaaS pricing impossible. Customer lifetime value is near-zero because high churn rates in restaurant industry. Unit economics (LTV, CAC, contribution margin) are unfavorable compared to e-commerce.

**Biggest insight:** *"Business viability trumps technical execution. A perfectly engineered system for a broken business model creates zero value. I learned that business model validity and unit economics are more important than code quality or UI aesthetics."*

**How this changed my thinking:** This insight reframed my entire approach to business development. I now evaluate business models by their unit economics and market viability—not by technical complexity or feature count. Technical excellence is valuable only when applied to a viable business model. This connects Entrepreneurial Judgment (evaluating opportunity cost vs revenue potential) and Financial Management (understanding unit economics).

**Second insight:** *"The gap between technical execution and strategic thinking is not about skill difference—it's about decision frameworks. I can be technically excellent and build sophisticated systems while still making terrible business decisions if I lack systematic validation."*

I realized I need frameworks for business model analysis—SaaS unit economics, competitive analysis, and market validation—not just "building better apps." This connects to Entrepreneurial Judgment by providing structured approaches for evaluating viability and informs Financial Management through unit economics understanding.

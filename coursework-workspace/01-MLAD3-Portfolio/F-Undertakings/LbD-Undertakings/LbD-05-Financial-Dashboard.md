# LbD Undertaking 5: Financial Dashboard Creation - Confronting Revenue Avoidance

**Connected Competency:** Financial Management
**Connected SMART Objective:** #4 (£5K+ Monthly Recurring Revenue by December 2025)
**Activity Period:** November 15 - December 10, 2025 (26 days)
**Word Count:** 650-750 words

## THE EXPERIENCE (What I did)

### Activity Overview

From November 15-30, I designed and built a financial dashboard to track revenue, expenses, and unit economics across my ventures. This was the first time I had created systematic financial visibility - previously, I had avoided looking at the numbers entirely. The dashboard tracks MRR (Monthly Recurring Revenue), CAC (Customer Acquisition Cost), LTV (Lifetime Value), and burn rate.

From December 1-10, I began using the dashboard to make actual business decisions: adjusting partnership pricing based on unit economics analysis, identifying unprofitable activities, and reallocating resources toward revenue-generating work.

### What Was Challenging

The psychological challenge was immense. For months, I had been "focusing on systems" while avoiding revenue. Building the dashboard forced me to confront: **Zero revenue.**

Specific challenges:
- **Facing the numbers**: Seeing £0 MRR on the dashboard I built was emotionally difficult
- **Admitting the pattern**: The dashboard made visible what I had been avoiding - I'd spent 80% of my time on system optimization and 20% on revenue generation
- **Money discomfort**: I discovered I have underlying discomfort with "chasing revenue" - it feels transactional and somehow less noble than "building systems"
- **Pricing uncertainty**: The dashboard revealed I had no validated unit economics - my £5K/month goal was arbitrary, not based on actual customer willingness to pay

### Unexpected Outcomes

The biggest surprise: **Visibility changed behavior immediately.**

Before the dashboard, I could tell myself "I'm making progress on systems, revenue will come later." After the dashboard, the £0 MRR metric stared at me every time I opened the app. It became impossible to ignore.

Within 48 hours of having the dashboard live:
- I restructured my daily schedule to prioritize partnership outreach (revenue-generating) over internal tool development (systems)
- I stopped work on two internal projects that had no revenue path
- I reached out to three prospective clients for paid work

The dashboard didn't just SHOW me the problem - it made the problem impossible to avoid.

Another surprise: The act of building the dashboard taught me what I didn't know. I couldn't complete the "Unit Economics" section because I had never actually tested pricing with real customers. The empty cells in the dashboard were as revealing as the filled ones.

## REFLECTION (What I learned from doing)

### What This Revealed About My Relationship with Money

I had been telling myself a story: "I'm building systems first, revenue will come naturally." The dashboard revealed this as avoidance.

Specifically:
- **Revenue avoidance**: I find technical work comfortable and certain; revenue work feels uncertain and exposes me to rejection
- **"Avoiding couple grand" pattern**: I now recognize that I've historically avoided small revenue targets while telling myself I'm "aiming bigger" - this is rationalization
- **Systems as shelter**: Building internal systems feels productive and defensible; revenue work can feel like "selling" which I have negative associations with

The dashboard forced confrontation: I could see exactly how many hours went into systems vs. revenue. The ratio was approximately 85%/15%. For someone whose goal is £5K/month MRR by December, this is misalignment.

### Connection to Theory: [Lean Startup - Innovation Accounting](https://theleanstartup.com/principles)

Eric Ries emphasizes "innovation accounting" - measuring the right metrics for startups, not vanity metrics. I had been measuring "features shipped" and "systems built" - vanity metrics that felt productive but didn't measure business health.

The dashboard shifted measurement to **validated learning metrics**:
- MRR (reality check: are we building something people will pay for?)
- CAC (how much does it cost to acquire a customer?)
- LTV (how much is each customer worth?)
- Unit economics (is this business model viable at scale?)

Ries argues that startups die from "vanity metrics" - measuring things that make you feel productive but don't predict success. My September-October focus on "features built" was exactly this anti-pattern. The dashboard moved me to real metrics.

### Connection to Theory: [Management by Wandering Around (MBWA)](https://hbr.org/1982/03/manage-by-wandering-around)

While MBWA typically refers to walking the factory floor, the principle applies: **leaders must have direct contact with reality, not filtered reports.**

For months, I had a "sense" of my financial situation without actual visibility. This is like a CEO reading summaries written by middle managers rather than seeing the factory floor. The dashboard is my MBWA - direct, unfiltered contact with financial reality.

The principle: What gets measured gets managed. By making financial metrics visible and impossible to ignore, the dashboard changed what I paid attention to.

### Skills Developed

**Financial Visibility**: Learned to design dashboards that show business health at a glance, not just raw data.

**Unit Economics Thinking**: Started asking "What are the economics of ONE partnership?" rather than "How do I get to 1,000 partnerships?"

**Self-Awareness**: Confronted my revenue avoidance pattern and money discomfort - the first step to changing it.

## APPLICATION (How I've used this learning)

### Changes in Behavior (December onwards)

The dashboard created immediate behavior change:

1. **Daily Revenue Review**: Every morning, I check MRR and revenue-generating activities from the previous day. If I spent the day on systems with no revenue impact, the dashboard makes that visible.

2. **Resource Reallocation**: Activities that don't show clear revenue impact in the dashboard get deprioritized. Internal tools are now built only when they directly accelerate revenue generation.

3. **Pricing Discipline**: Before the dashboard, my £5K/month goal was arbitrary. Now, I'm testing actual pricing with prospects to fill in the unit economics section with REAL data, not guesses.

### Concrete Evidence of Application

**Before dashboard (Nov 1-14):**
- Time on systems: 85%
- Time on revenue: 15%
- MRR: £0
- Financial visibility: Zero (hadn't looked at numbers in months)

**After dashboard (Dec 1-10):**
- Time on systems: 40%
- Time on revenue: 60%
- Prospects contacted: 3 (with pricing proposals)
- Financial visibility: Daily dashboard review
- Unit economics: Active testing (pricing conversations in progress)

The specific outcome: On December 5, when I felt the urge to build another internal automation tool, I opened the dashboard, saw £0 MRR, and instead spent 4 hours reaching out to prospective partners. The dashboard reframed the decision: "Technical work that feels productive" vs. "Revenue work that moves the metric."

### Future Applications

The dashboard will evolve as the business scales:
- **January 2026**: Add partnership economics (revenue per partner, churn rate)
- **February 2026**: Add cohort analysis (which partnership sources convert best)
- **March 2026**: Add cash flow forecasting (when will we need external funding?)

For the £5K/month goal specifically: The dashboard creates the feedback loop needed to validate whether the partnership business model actually works. If MRR doesn't hit £5K by December, the dashboard will show exactly where the bottleneck is (conversion rate? Pricing? Partnership volume?).

## VALIDATION (Why this learning matters)

### Strengths of This Approach

Honest about avoidance: I'm admitting that I avoided revenue for months, not portraying myself as naturally strategic.

Psychological insight: The dashboard revealed not just business metrics but my relationship with money and revenue.

Concrete outcome: Within 48 hours of building the dashboard, I restructured my time allocation. The visibility created behavior change.

### Limitations of What I Learned

The dashboard shows I have zero revenue - but doesn't solve the problem. I still need to do the hard work of customer development, pricing validation, and actual sales.

Also, the dashboard is only as good as the data that goes into it. I haven't yet tested pricing with real customers, so the unit economics section is empty. The next step is running pricing experiments to fill those gaps.

### Relevance to Exit Strategy

This is foundational: The 1,000 partnership goal requires a sustainable business model. I can't scale to 1,000 partnerships without understanding unit economics, customer acquisition cost, and lifetime value.

The dashboard is the tool that will tell me whether the partnership model actually works. If unit economics don't pencil out at 10 partnerships, they definitely won't work at 1,000. The dashboard provides early warning.

More deeply, this represents the shift from "systems founder" to "business founder." Building systems is comfortable; building a profitable business requires confronting revenue, pricing, and customer economics. The dashboard is my tool for that confrontation.

November 8-18, 2025 marked my first significant e-commerce client delivery. Unlike the restaurant app before it, this project taught me the critical difference between "building what I'm told" and "discovering what the client actually needs through iteration."

The project was scoped as a landing page with Shopify backend integration and mobile app functionality - but the real learning happened through the 7-8 rounds of feedback and iteration.

**What I needed to learn:**

- That client feedback about their business is more accurate than my technical assumptions
- That iteration isn't failure - it's the discovery process
- That "utility over elegance" matters more to clients than technical perfection
- That the restaurant app lessons directly transferred to e-commerce

## 6. Critical Evaluation of Learning

### **What?**

From November 8-18, 2025, I delivered an e-commerce project for a £1,000 paying client, implementing a landing page with Shopify backend integration and mobile app functionality. The project involved 7-8 rounds of iterative feedback where I implemented all client requests, learning that the client's understanding of their business and customers was more accurate than my technical assumptions.

The skills and competencies developed include: **Service Design & Delivery** through iterative client-centric development, **Professional Communication** through requirements gathering and feedback management, and **Entrepreneurial Judgment** through value-based pricing and scope management.

### **So what?**

**What challenged my assumptions:**

I assumed that as the technical expert, I would know what features and functionality the client needed. I was wrong. The client's feedback consistently revealed that they understood their customers' behavior, preferences, and needs better than I did. When I suggested what I thought were "obvious" features, the client often corrected me with explanations like "My customers don't use mobile apps for that" or "That would confuse my checkout flow."

**Biggest insight:** **"Client feedback about their business is market intelligence. My technical assumptions are hypotheses to be tested."**

This was a profound shift from my previous perfectionist patterns. In September-October, I would have spent weeks refining features I assumed were important. With this e-commerce client, I built quickly, got feedback, and iterated. The client's guidance was more accurate than my speculation - every time.

**Second insight:** **"Iteration isn't failure - it's the discovery process for requirements."**

The 7-8 rounds of feedback weren't "rework" or "mistakes" - they were the actual requirements discovery process. The client couldn't articulate everything they needed upfront. They discovered requirements through seeing each iteration. My role wasn't to "get it right the first time" - it was to facilitate the discovery process through rapid iteration.

**Third insight:** **"Domain learning from restaurants transferred to e-commerce. Technical patterns, market understanding didn't."**

The technical skills from the restaurant app (payment integration, mobile app architecture, order management systems) transferred directly to e-commerce. But the market understanding (pricing, customer behavior, feature priorities) didn't transfer. I had to learn e-commerce specifics from the client. This validated my strategic pivot: technical skills are portable, but market understanding is niche-specific.

### **Now what?**

**How I applied this learning:**

**Requirements gathering approach:** I now start every client project with discovery questions before proposing solutions. "What business problem does this solve?" "How will you measure success?" "What have you tried before?" This prevents me from building what I assume is needed.

**Iteration as standard:** I no longer present iteration rounds as "revisions" or "fixes" - they're part of the discovery process. I set expectations with clients upfront: "We'll iterate based on your feedback until it's right." This changes the relationship from "delivering to spec" to "discovering together."

**Technical confidence, business humility:** I'm confident in my technical execution ability, but humble about business requirements. The client is the expert on their business and customers. My role is to enable their vision through technology, not to replace their judgment with my assumptions.

**Future application:**

As I scale to 1,000 partnerships, many will be with agencies serving e-commerce brands. This project gave me direct experience with e-commerce needs, Shopify ecosystem challenges, and what e-commerce businesses value. I can now speak credibly to e-commerce agency partners about their clients' needs because I've worked directly with an e-commerce client.

Additionally, the iterative delivery model (quick build → feedback → iterate) is the same approach I'll use for partnership onboarding. I won't build perfect partnership systems in isolation - I'll build quickly, test with partners, and iterate based on feedback.

## 3. VALIDITY: SOURCE ASSESSMENT (900 words)

### Core Sources (Top 6 - Most Critical)

**Source 1: "The Lean Startup" by Eric Ries (Re-read for financial focus)**
- **Type:** Book / Startup methodology
- **Date:** 2011 (re-read 2025 for revenue validation)
- **Validity Rating:** 9.5/10 (validated by thousands of startups)
- **Key Insight:** Ries' concept of "validated learning about customers" applies equally to revenue. Don't build systems hoping revenue will come - validate revenue FIRST, then build systems to support it. My September-October pattern was the exact opposite: build systems, hope for revenue later
- **Relevance:** This directly confronted my "systems first" approach. The financial dashboard I built (LbD-5) was an application of Ries' innovation accounting - measuring real metrics (MRR, unit economics) rather than vanity metrics (features built)

**Source 2: "$100M Offers" by Alex Hormozi**
- **Type:** Book / Pricing and value proposition framework
- **Date:** 2021
- **Validity Rating:** 9/10 (proven business results, practical frameworks)
- **Key Insight:** Value equation: **Value = (Dream Outcome × Success Probability × Time Delay × Effort × Sacrifice) / Price**. Most people undercharge because they focus on their costs/hours, not the customer's perceived value. The "dream outcome" multiplier means solving painful problems is worth exponentially more than mild improvements
- **Relevance:** This transformed my pricing approach from "cost-plus" (my hours × rate) to "value-based" (percentage of value delivered). One partnership proposal is priced at "10% of revenue increase" rather than a fixed fee - directly applying Hormozi's framework

**Source 3: SaaS Unit Economics Research (Christoph Janz, David Cummings, etc.)**
- **Type:** Blog posts and research by SaaS investors
- **URL:** christophjanz.blogspot.com, davidgcummings.com
- **Date:** 2023-2025
- **Validity Rating:** 9/10 (data-driven, real-world SaaS metrics)
- **Key Insight:** Healthy SaaS unit economics: LTV:CAC ratio of 3:1 or better, CAC payback under 12 months, net dollar retention above 100%. If these metrics don't work at small scale, they won't work at large scale. The time to validate unit economics is NOW, at 10 customers, not later at 1,000
- **Relevance:** This created urgency around pricing validation. I can't scale to 1,000 partnerships without first validating that unit economics work for the first 10. The dashboard I built tracks exactly these metrics.

**Source 4: "The Mom Test" by Rob Fitzpatrick (Re-read for pricing conversations)**
- **Type:** Book / Customer development methodology
- **Date:** 2013 (re-read 2025)
- **Validity Rating:** 9/10 (practical, immediately applicable)
- **Key Insight:** When discussing pricing, don't ask "Would you pay £X?" (people lie). Instead, ask "What are you currently paying for this problem?" and "What would it be worth to solve this?" Actions reveal true value, not opinions
- **Relevance:** This transformed my pricing conversations. Instead of proposing a price, I now ask about current spend and problem cost, then price based on revealed value. This reveals actual willingness to pay rather than hypothetical

**Source 5: "Predictably Irrational" by Dan Ariely**
- **Type:** Book / Behavioral economics
- **Date:** 2008
- **Validity Rating:** 8.5/10 (well-researched, relevant to pricing psychology)
- **Key Insight:** Humans don't have an innate sense of "fair price" - we determine value by comparison and anchoring. The first price you hear becomes the anchor. This means there's no "objectively correct" price - pricing is about framing and anchoring, not cost-plus calculation
- **Relevance:** This liberated me from "cost-plus" pricing. If value is subjective and anchored by framing, I should anchor high and frame in terms of outcomes, not hours. My partnership pricing now anchors at "£5K-£10K/month" rather than starting low

**Source 6: HubSpot and Shopify Pricing Strategy Analysis (Case Studies)**
- **Type:** Public company pricing analysis
- **URL:** investor relations documents, pricing pages
- **Date:** 2024-2025
- **Validity Rating:** 8.5/10 (real-world pricing data, validated by market)
- **Key Insight:** Both companies use **freemium + tiered pricing** to (a) lower friction to start, (b) capture value as customers grow, and (c) create clear upgrade paths. The "free" tier isn't charity - it's a customer acquisition channel with known conversion rates
- **Relevance:** This informed my partnership tier structure: Test tier (free validation), Growth tier (monthly revenue share), Scale tier (higher percentage of larger revenue). The tiered structure creates an upgrade path as agencies succeed

## 4. CRITICAL EVALUATION OF LEARNING (780 words)

From October to December 2025, I engaged in learning on business models, pricing, and financial thinking:

**October:** Understanding business model fundamentals
- Re-read "The Lean Startup" focusing on revenue validation
- Studied SaaS unit economics research (Janz, Cummings, others)

**November:** Pricing and value-based thinking

- Re-read "The Mom Test" for pricing conversations

**December:** Application and financial visibility
- Built financial dashboard (LbD-5) tracking MRR, unit economics
- Applied value-based pricing to partnership proposals
- Validated pricing through customer conversations
- Shifted time allocation from systems to revenue work

### SO WHAT? (How This Changed My Thinking)

**Initial Assumption Challenged:** I believed that "building great systems" was the hard work and "generating revenue" would naturally follow once systems were perfect.

**What I Discovered:** Revenue doesn't follow from systems - revenue must be validated FIRST, then systems are built to support validated revenue. My September-October pattern was exactly backwards: build systems, hope revenue comes. The correct sequence is: validate revenue, build systems to support it.

**Biggest Insight:** **"Pricing is not about your costs or hours - it's about the value you create for the customer."**

Hormozi's value equation was transformative. I had been pricing based on "my hours × my rate" - a cost-plus approach that caps revenue at my capacity. Value-based pricing uncouples revenue from my hours: "10% of revenue increase" means I earn more when the customer earns more. Our incentives are aligned.

**Second Insight:** **"Unit economics that don't work at 10 customers won't work at 1,000."**

I had been thinking "I'll figure out pricing later, once I have more customers." SaaS research revealed this as backwards - unit economics must be validated at small scale or they never become viable. The dashboard I built (LbD-5) tracks exactly these metrics (LTV, CAC, payback period) because they're make-or-break for the business.

**Third Insight:** **"Financial visibility changes behavior."**

Before the dashboard, I could tell myself "I'm making progress on systems." After the dashboard, the £0 MRR metric stared at me daily, making it impossible to ignore. Within 48 hours of building the dashboard, I restructured my time allocation (LbD-5) from 85% systems / 15% revenue to 40% systems / 60% revenue. Visibility forced behavior change.

### NOW WHAT? (Application and Integration)

**What I Changed Based on This Learning:**

1. **Financial Dashboard** (November 15 - December 10, 2025)
   - Built dashboard tracking MRR, CAC, LTV, unit economics, burn rate
   - Daily review creates financial visibility
   - Metrics inform strategic decisions about where to focus
   - Direct application of innovation accounting from "The Lean Startup"

2. **Pricing Strategy Shift** (November-December 2025)
   - **Before:** Cost-plus pricing (my hours × rate)
   - **After:** Value-based pricing (percentage of value delivered)
   - Example: "10% of revenue increase" vs. fixed fee
   - Anchored at £5K-£10K/month (learned from pricing psychology)

3. **Time Allocation Restructure** (December 2025)
   - **Before:** 85% systems / 15% revenue (September-October)
   - **After:** 40% systems / 60% revenue (December onwards)
   - Dashboard made misalignment visible and impossible to ignore
   - Protected hours for revenue work (partnerships, client work)

4. **Validation Approach** (December 2025)
   - Pricing conversations start with "What are you currently paying for this problem?"
   - Test willingness to pay before building complete solutions
   - Validate unit economics at 3-10 partnerships before scaling to 100-1,000
   - Apply "Mom Test" principles to pricing conversations

**How This Has Already Impacted My Decisions:**

**Stopped Systems-First Work (December):** When I felt the urge to build another internal automation tool, the dashboard's £0 MRR metric stopped me. Instead, I spent 4 hours reaching out to prospective partners (revenue work).

**Partnership Pricing (December):** Instead of quoting a fixed fee, I now have a conversation about current spend and problem cost, then price as a percentage of value. One proposal is "£1K/month + 10% of revenue increase" - value-based pricing.

**Client Work Priority (December):** When deciding between internal tool development vs. client work, the dashboard makes visible that client work advances MRR while internal tools don't. The dashboard decides: client work wins.

**Future Application:**

The dashboard and value-based pricing approach will scale with the business:
- **January 2026:** Add partnership economics (revenue per partner, churn)
- **February 2026:** Add cohort analysis (which partnerships convert best)
- **March 2026:** Add cash flow forecasting

The principle "validate revenue first, build systems second" will inform all strategic decisions. Every new initiative will be evaluated: "Does this validate or advance revenue, or is it systems-for-systems-sake?"

**4. Accountability:** AI helped me confront uncomfortable truths. When I described my September-October pattern, AI confirmed: "This is the anti-pattern. You're building without validating revenue." AI's objectivity was uncomfortable but necessary.

### How I Felt About Using AI

**Positives:**

**Overall Assessment:**

The strategic insights (value-based pricing, revenue-first validation, unit economics) came from synthesis across human experts (Ries, Hormozi, Janz) - AI helped me apply those insights immediately through dashboard design, pricing proposals, and financial modeling.
